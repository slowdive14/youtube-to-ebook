# The Time Travelers’ Toolkit: Why Resurrecting Lost Technologies is the Future of History

Traditional archaeology is often a quiet, painstaking affair. It conjures images of researchers in khaki shorts, meticulously brushing dust off pot shards or measuring the foundation of a long-collapsed wall. This "armchair" approach tells us what the past looked like, but it leaves the other four senses wanting. What did the ancient world sound like? How did it taste? What did it feel like to hold its tools or wear its fashions?

According to author Sam Kean, the answer lies in **experimental archaeology**—a field where researchers stop just looking at artifacts and start recreating them. By resurrecting lost technologies, from Stone Age beds to Roman concrete and Viking medicine, we are discovering that our ancestors were far more sophisticated than we ever dared to imagine.

## The Ash Mattress and the Ostrich Canteen

Imagine yourself in Africa 75,000 years ago. You are nomadic, moving between caves with hard, unforgiving dirt floors. How do you get a good night's sleep? 

Experimental archaeologists have found that early humans didn't just pile up leaves; they engineered their environment. First, they would burn debris in the cave to create a thick, soft layer of ash. This served as a "memory foam" mattress. Next, they would lay down aromatic, medicinal leaves. 

When researchers recreated these beds, they discovered a hidden genius: the ash layer killed ticks by gumming up their mouthparts, while the aromatic leaves acted as a natural mosquito repellent. "It was as comfortable as sleeping on a beach," Kean notes, proving that even tens of thousands of years ago, humans were masters of domestic comfort and pest control.

The sophistication extended to their "hardware." To carry water, ancient people used ostrich eggs. By sawing a small hole with stone tools and cleaning the interior with herbs, they created canteens that held six cups of liquid. Because ostrich shells are slightly porous, they allow for microscopic evaporation—a process that keeps the water inside colder than the outside air. These weren't just containers; they were self-cooling refrigerators.

## The Secret Ingredient in Roman Ruins

We often wonder why modern bridges crumble after fifty years while Roman aqueducts and the Pantheon stand firm after two millennia. For a long time, archaeologists thought the Romans were simply sloppy. They noticed white chunks of "unmixed" lime in Roman concrete and assumed it was the result of poor craftsmanship.

However, researchers at MIT recently used experimental methods to prove the opposite. Those lime clasts were a deliberate feature. When a crack forms in Roman concrete and water seeps in, it reacts with those "undigested" chunks of lime, creating a solution that recrystallizes and fills the crack. It is **self-healing concrete**. This technology allowed Roman infrastructure to survive seismic activity and environmental decay that would have leveled a modern skyscraper.

## The Hairdresser Who Revolutionized Classics

One of the most remarkable breakthroughs in Roman history didn't come from a PhD candidate, but from a professional hairdresser in Baltimore named Janet Stevens. 

While visiting a museum, Stevens became obsessed with the gravity-defying hairstyles of Roman matrons depicted in marble busts. Traditional scholars had long argued that these complex "mountains of curls" were wigs. Stevens, knowing the physics of hair, disagreed. She spent years trying to recreate the styles with modern pins and products, but she failed.

Refusing to give up, she taught herself enough Latin to translate ancient texts. She eventually found a reference to a "needle" used by hairdressers. The epiphany followed: Roman women weren't pinning their hair; they were **sewing** it. Using a needle and thread, Stevens was able to perfectly replicate the most complex styles of antiquity. Her work was eventually published in top-tier archaeological journals, proving that practical, "hands-on" knowledge can solve mysteries that have baffled academics for centuries.

## Dining with King Tut: Bread and "Kombucha" Beer

Kean’s journey into the past also took him to the kitchen. In ancient Egypt, bread and beer were the staples of life. To understand the Egyptian palate, researchers went to extreme lengths, sourcing heirloom emmer grains and even culturing 4,000-year-old yeast found in ancient pottery.

The result? "The best loaf of bread I have ever eaten in my life," Kean says. Despite having only four ingredients—emmer, coriander, salt, and yeast—the bread was perfectly balanced with a springy crust. 

The beer, however, was a surprise. Because the Egyptians didn't use hops (which provide the bitterness in modern IPAs), their beer tasted more like a tart, refreshing sour or a modern kombucha. In the blistering heat of the Sahara, this acidic, probiotic-rich drink was likely more about hydration and health than intoxication.

## Viking Antibiotics and the Biofilm Breakthrough

Perhaps the most startling discovery in experimental archaeology involves the "Leechbook," a 10th-century Viking medical text. It contained a recipe for an eye salve consisting of onions, garlic, wine, and ox gall (cow bile), brewed in a copper vessel for nine days.

Modern scientists, skeptical of "medieval superstition," decided to test the concoction against modern superbugs like MRSA (drug-resistant staph). To their shock, the mixture didn't just kill the bacteria; it was more effective than many modern antibiotics. 

Crucially, the "Viking antibiotic" was able to penetrate **biofilms**—the protective slime layers that bacteria use to shield themselves from medicine. This ancient recipe is now being developed as a modern drug, proving that "lost" knowledge might be the key to surviving the post-antibiotic era.

## The Shadow War: The Hunt for Hitler’s Bomb

The second half of Kean’s investigation moves from the ancient world to the high-stakes espionage of World War II. The "Alsos Mission" was a real-life "Rogue One" style operation where scientists and soldiers went behind enemy lines to sabotage the Nazi atomic bomb project.

The mission was fueled by a "game of telephone." When the brilliant German physicist Werner Heisenberg had a lab accident—a chemical explosion caused by uranium reacting with water—rumors reached the Allies that the Nazis had achieved a successful atomic chain reaction. This sparked a panic that accelerated the Manhattan Project.

The Alsos Mission recruited unlikely heroes, including **Moe Berg**, a Major League Baseball catcher who spoke half a dozen languages and was friends with Albert Einstein. Berg was sent into Europe with a pistol and a cyanide pill, tasked with attending a lecture by Heisenberg and assassinating him if he suspected the Germans were close to a bomb. 

While the mission eventually discovered that the Nazis were nowhere near a functional weapon, the effort led to the seizure of tons of uranium and the recruitment of German scientists, fundamentally shaping the Cold War.

## Conclusion: A Living History

Experimental archaeology reminds us that history is not a linear progression from "primitive" to "advanced." We have forgotten as much as we have learned. Whether it’s the self-cooling ostrich egg or the self-healing Roman road, the technologies of the past offer elegant, sustainable solutions to problems we still struggle with today.

By tasting the bread of the Pharaohs and feeling the sting of an obsidian blade, we do more than just study the past—we experience the shared ingenuity of the human species. As Sam Kean demonstrates, the secrets of our future may very well be buried in the "lost" habits of our ancestors.

# The Intelligence Allocators: Why the Next Generation of Great Companies Will Be AI-Native

In the history of business, the "greats" have always been defined by their ability to allocate resources better than their competitors. Warren Buffett became a legend by mastering the allocation of capital. McKinsey & Company built a global empire by mastering the allocation of talent. 

But according to Rem Koning, a professor at Harvard Business School who specializes in entrepreneurship and artificial intelligence, we are entering a new era. In this landscape, the defining competitive advantage won’t just be money or people—it will be the ability to **allocate intelligence.**

"We’re in a world where increasingly what matters is your ability to work out what is done by which model," Koning explains. "How do you blend, orchestrate, and allocate your product to these different sorts of intelligences? If you can work that out better than other people, you’ve got an edge in the market."

## The Paradox of the Digital Assistant

The promise of AI has long been framed as a "great equalizer"—a tool that allows a coconut seller in Indonesia or a solo founder in Nairobi to compete with a Silicon Valley mogul. However, Koning’s research reveals a more nuanced, and perhaps more sobering, reality.

Three years ago, Koning conducted a study in Kenya, delivering GPT-based business advice to small-scale entrepreneurs via WhatsApp. The goal was to see if AI could bridge the performance gap for struggling businesses. The results were startling: while high-performing entrepreneurs saw their profits rise, the entrepreneurs who were already struggling saw a **10% decline** in revenues and profits after using the AI.

The reason? A lack of judgment.

"The AI gives them four or five plausible things to do," Koning says. "The low performers didn't have the mental models to separate the good advice from the bad. They followed the 'slop,' and it led them down a road of lower profitability."

This highlights a critical truth for the AI era: AI is an amplifier, not just an equalizer. It makes the good better, but it can make the unprepared worse. Access to the tool is no longer the bottleneck; the bottleneck is the human "earned insight" required to guide it.

## Defining the "AI-Native" Firm

We often hear the term "AI-native," but what does it actually mean in practice? For Koning, it isn't just about using ChatGPT to write emails or Claude to debug code. It’s about a fundamental shift in how a company scales.

Take the presentation software **Gamma** as an example. In a pre-generative AI world, a company offering high-end, custom-designed slide decks would have needed to employ thousands of graphic designers. The economics would have collapsed under the weight of headcount. 

"The key to being AI-native is that you’re not just using AI to do the work; you’re embedding it in the product so the AI can directly do the work with the customer," Koning explains. "You want to take the human out of the loop. I love humans, but the problem with humans is we don't scale particularly well."

By scaling with **compute** rather than **headcount**, AI-native firms can achieve margins and growth trajectories that were previously impossible. They operate on a "loop" where the user interacts directly with the intelligence, allowing the core team to remain lean while the output remains massive.

## The Death of the Chatbot and the Rise of the Agent

For the past two years, the world has been obsessed with the chatbot interface. Koning argues that this is a transitional phase—and one we need to move past quickly. 

"We don't need more chatbots," Koning asserts. "If a chatbot tells a Kenyan entrepreneur they need to update their website to get more sales, but that entrepreneur doesn't know how to code, the advice is useless."

The future lies in **agentic AI**—systems that don't just talk, but *act*. Instead of a chatbot giving advice, Koning envisions "virtual employees" that execute the marketing plan, build the website, and manage the CRM. This shift is particularly vital for founders outside of major tech hubs who may lack access to specialized local labor. 

If a founder in Nairobi can "hire" a virtual agent as effective as a New York City marketing executive for the cost of a few API calls, the global economic landscape changes overnight.

## The "Vibe Coding" Economy and Niche Software

One of the most exciting shifts Koning identifies is the democratization of software creation through what is being called "vibe coding"—using natural language and multimodal tools to build functional applications without traditional engineering backgrounds.

This lowers the barrier to entry so significantly that software can now tackle "mundane" problems that were previously too small to be profitable for VC-backed startups. 

"Imagine a restaurant owner in Thailand who needs a very specific CRM for their business," Koning says. "Now, someone can take a tool like Lovable or Codex and build the world’s best CRM for Thai restaurant owners. It solves a specific problem, and because the marginal cost of software is low, it can help millions."

This suggests a move away from the "winner-take-all" network effects of the Facebook era and toward a proliferation of small, highly efficient, bootstrapped SaaS businesses that spread prosperity more broadly.

## The Founder’s Trap: AI is Not a Substitute for Strategy

Despite the power of these new tools, Koning warns of a dangerous trap: the assumption that building with AI automatically means building something people want.

"I’m seeing a loop where people get stuck because the AI tools are so fun," he says. "You tell Claude to make something, then add a feature, then another. A month goes by and you’ve built a beautiful piece of over-engineered software that nobody wants."

The traditional rules of entrepreneurship still apply. A "drop or two" of AI in the right place is often more valuable than an AI-heavy product that lacks a core user insight. The most successful founders will be those who use AI to solve a specific, hard-earned problem rather than those who simply chase the latest model's capabilities.

## A Shift in Ambition

As we look forward, the relationship between humans and machines may undergo a final, radical shift. Drew Bent, who leads education at Anthropic, suggests that we are currently under-utilizing the technology by giving it simple tasks.

"We are not elevating our ambition with what we can do with these AI tools," Bent notes. He envisions an "inversion of control" where, in some cases, the AI model handles the high-level strategic thinking and delegates specific tasks back to the human for areas that require "human taste and agency."

For Rem Koning, the message to entrepreneurs is clear: the world is becoming more entrepreneurial because the cost of "doing" is dropping to zero. In such a world, the only thing that remains expensive—and therefore valuable—is the ability to think, to judge, and to allocate intelligence effectively.

"The world is going to look a lot more entrepreneurial," Koning concludes. "And if you can work out how to blend your human intelligence with these models better than anyone else, you’ve got the ultimate edge."

# The Architects of Abundance: Joel Mokyr on the Hidden Roots of Human Progress

In the modern era, we are remarkably quick to habituate to miracles. We receive a device that can map the entire globe and translate any language in real-time, and within a week, we are complaining that the battery dies too quickly or the 5G signal is spotty in the elevator. We are, as a species, professionally ungrateful.

Joel Mokyr, a legendary economic historian at Northwestern University and a recent Nobel laureate, views it as his mission to remind us just how miserable the "good old days" actually were. "The good old days may have been old," Mokyr says, "but they weren't good. They were terrible."

In a wide-ranging conversation on *Freakonomics Radio*, Mokyr laid out a provocative thesis: economic progress isn’t just about money, laws, or resources. It is driven by a tiny sliver of the population—perhaps just two or three percent—who possess a specific cultural mindset. These are the people who believe that the world can be understood, that nature can be harnessed, and that "useful knowledge" is the ultimate lever for human flourishing.

### The Problem with GDP

To understand progress, Mokyr argues we must first stop obsessing over GDP. Gross Domestic Product was designed to measure business cycles and year-to-year fluctuations; it is a blunt instrument for measuring human welfare over centuries. 

Consider the invention of anesthesia. Before the mid-19th century, surgery was a nightmare of trauma. A surgeon had roughly two minutes to perform an amputation before the patient died of sheer shock. Today, we undergo surgery in a chemical slumber. 

"How much would you have to be paid in order to have your appendix taken out without anesthesia?" Mokyr asks. Most would say no amount is enough. Yet, in the ledger of GDP, anesthesia is a "drop in the ocean." Similarly, the GPS on your phone, digital photography, and free communication apps provide massive "consumer surplus"—value we enjoy without paying for it—that never shows up in official economic growth statistics. 

### Why the "Hockey Stick" Happened

For most of human history, the graph of human prosperity was a flat line. Then, around 1800, it turned into a "hockey stick," shooting upward at an unprecedented angle. What caused the break?

Mainstream economists often point to **institutions**—the "rules of the game," such as property rights and the rule of law. While Mokyr agrees institutions are vital, he argues they are secondary to **culture**.

"The explosion of technological progress in the West was made possible by cultural changes," Mokyr explains. It wasn't just that people had better laws; it was that they changed their attitude toward the natural world. Influenced by thinkers like Francis Bacon, a small group of "upper-tail" intellectuals convinced society that the systematic application of science to technology was the path to wealth.

This led to the "Great Convergence" of the late 19th century. Once we understood the physics of electricity, we got the lightbulb. Once we mastered organic chemistry, we got modern medicine and fertilizers. Once we figured out how to make steel cheap and reliable (the Bessemer process), we got the skyscrapers and steamships that shrunk the world.

### The "Upper-Tail" Theory of History

One of Mokyr’s more controversial stances is that progress is an elitist affair. He argues that the vast majority of people throughout history did not contribute to the breakthroughs that define the modern world. Instead, progress is driven by a "very small proportion of the labor force"—the scientists, tinkerers, and engineers who occupy the far right tail of the talent distribution.

"Is literature created by the masses? No, it’s written by the few," Mokyr says. "The same is true for sports, painting, and music." 

While this may sound anti-egalitarian, Mokyr views it as a win-win. We don't need everyone to be a Louis Pasteur or a Robert Koch to benefit from the germ theory of disease. The genius of the few provides the "useful knowledge" that lifts the life expectancy of the many. In 1800, the average life expectancy was around 35; today, in Europe, it is 82. That is a gift from the "upper tail" to the rest of humanity.

### The Culture of Failure

If progress depends on innovation, then society must be structured to handle the inevitable byproduct of innovation: failure. 

Mokyr points to Israel as a modern case study. When he arrived there in the 1950s, the country was poor, the food was "barely edible," and the infrastructure was crumbling. Today, Israel is a high-tech powerhouse with a GDP per capita higher than Italy’s. 

The secret? A culture where failing is allowed. "As somebody once said famously, it's not that in Israel people think outside the box; it is that there is no box." 

He notes a historical parallel in 18th-century Britain. One reason the Industrial Revolution took hold there was the "Poor Law." Because the local parish ensured that people wouldn't starve, entrepreneurs were more willing to take risks. A safety net, counterintuitively, can be a trampoline for technological dynamism.

### The "Unforced Errors" of the Present

Despite his historical optimism, Mokyr is deeply worried about the 21st century. He identifies several "unforced errors" that threaten to stall the engine of progress.

**1. The War on Immigration**
Mokyr calls the current Western hostility toward immigration "disgraceful and self-defeating." Immigrants, he notes, are disproportionately likely to be "upper-tail" individuals. They file more patents, and those patents are, on average, twice as valuable as those filed by native-born citizens. "The people who want to move to America... are people we should want. They want to work, they want to send their kids to college, they will pay taxes."

**2. Institutional Deterioration**
Mokyr distinguishes between **technological progress** (which is cumulative and usually moves forward) and **institutional change** (which can go backward). He fears we are entering an era where the rules-based international order is collapsing. When leaders like Vladimir Putin ignore the rule against annexing smaller neighbors, it sends a signal that the "barbarism of the past" is returning.

**3. The "Asteroid" Threats**
While Mokyr is sanguine about AI—believing it will revolutionize education and medicine—he is terrified of nuclear proliferation. He views nuclear weapons as the modern "asteroid" that could end the human experiment. "This is a world that is now nuclear-armed... that’s what really scares me."

### The Hardware Problem

As Mokyr reflects on his 50-year career, his optimism has been tempered. He sees a growing gap between our "capabilities" (our technology) and our "preferences" (our human nature). 

We are running 21st-century software on Stone Age hardware. We have the technology to feed, clothe, and heal everyone on earth, yet we remain trapped in zero-sum competitive drives that lead to war and tribalism. 

"Why is it that we continuously see people rising to positions of power who have no business being there?" he wonders. 

Ultimately, Mokyr’s work suggests that progress is not inevitable. It is a fragile flower that grows only in the soil of a specific culture—one that prizes knowledge, tolerates failure, and remains open to the world. We have inherited a world of unimaginable abundance, but if we lose the culture that built it, we may find ourselves clustering around the fireplace once again, wondering where it all went wrong.

# The Alchemist in the Bedroom: How Kova Uses an AI "Stack" to Turn Short-Form Video into Viral Gold

In the modern attention economy, a million views isn’t just a vanity metric—it’s the ultimate leverage. For founders, it’s the difference between a quiet launch and a sold-out startup. For creators, it’s the bridge between a hobby and a career. But as the digital landscape becomes saturated, the barrier to "going viral" has shifted from mere consistency to high-level artistry.

From her bedroom, she produces short-form content that blends "hacker culture" with "Kawaii nostalgia," racking up tens of millions of views. Her secret isn't a massive production crew; it’s a sophisticated, agentic AI workflow that allows a single person to produce Hollywood-level visuals.

In a recent deep dive with Greg Isenberg, Kova pulled back the curtain on the specific AI stack she uses to script, shoot, and stylize her way to the top of the algorithm.

---

## 1. The Agentic Breakdown: Reverse-Engineering Viral Success
Before Kova picks up a camera, she uses AI to "deconstruct" the DNA of successful content. While most creators try to mimic their idols through trial and error, Kova uses **Manis AI**, an agentic tool that can "watch" a video and provide a granular replication plan.

"I like putting the burden of proof on the AI," Kova explains. Unlike standard LLMs that might guess based on a URL, Manis operates the browser, parses the video frames, and extracts the exact "vibe" and structure.

### The "Tamagotchi" Case Study
When Manis analyzed one of Kova’s most successful videos—a devlog about a Tamagotchi flash drive—it broke the content down into a **Five-Act Structure**:
1.  **The Visual Hook:** A 10-second explanation of a technical mechanic with an immediate visual payoff.
2.  **Conflict & Stakes:** Introducing a personal challenge (e.g., "zero hardware experience") to build relatability.
3.  **The Build:** Three distinct steps punctuated by circle-numbered graphics.
4.  **Problem/Resolution:** A comedic dramatic arc (Too slow, too fast, just right).
5.  **The Tease/CTA:** Seeding the next episode.

By using AI to identify these beats, creators can build a "Style Guide" that includes everything from typography (using fonts like *Fredoka* for a rounded, friendly feel) to lighting (the "Dark Academia" aesthetic).

"No longer is Hollywood-level studio sets kept to those studios," Kova says. If her filming location feels bare, she doesn't move furniture; she uses AI to add "aesthetic clutter" that drives retention.

### The Process of "Visual Levelling"
Kova takes a static frame from her footage and brings it into an image editor. Using simple prompts, she annotates the image to add:
*   **Depth:** Adding fairy lights or a vinyl record player in the background.
*   **Balance:** Placing orange tulips in a vase or a plush toy on a windowsill to fill "dead space" in the frame.
*   **Lighting:** Enhancing the bokeh (background blur) to make a phone-shot video look like it was filmed on a $5,000 cinema camera.

**The Business Logic:** Why bother with an AI-generated plush toy? **Retention.** A more visually interesting frame keeps eyes on the screen for those crucial extra seconds, signaling to the Instagram or TikTok algorithm that the content is worth sharing with millions more.

## 3. Creating "Impossible" Transitions
The first 15 seconds of a video are the "make or break" moments. Kova uses AI video generators like **Luma Dream Machine, Kling, or C-Dance** to create transitions that would be impossible or prohibitively expensive to film traditionally.

For example, in a video about her journey as an artist, she used a childhood photo as a "Start Frame" and used AI to animate her younger self waving and laughing. She then stitched this AI-generated clip to her modern-day footage.

### Kova’s Pro-Tip for Video Prompting:
When using AI to generate these clips, Kova emphasizes being specific about the "camera" rather than just the "subject."
*   **Wrong:** "A kid waving."
*   **Right:** "Camera is stationary. On the table is a picture of a kid. The kid in the center of the picture is waving their arms gleefully while the picture frame remains static."

By describing the shot like a cinematographer, you avoid the "uncanny valley" morphing that often plagues amateur AI video.

---

## 4. Scaling the Art: The Obsidian & Claude System
As a creator’s output grows, "vibes" aren't enough—you need a system. Kova manages her entire creative output using **Obsidian** (a note-taking app) integrated with **Claude Code** or **Cursor**.

### The "Kova Cut" Style Guide
Kova maintains a digital vault in Obsidian that contains her "Kova Cut" style guide. This document defines her voice: "lowercase, casual, one idea per breath."

When she has a raw script, she doesn't manually storyboard it. She feeds the script into **Claude** and asks it to:
1.  Apply the "Kova Cut" style guide.
2.  Convert the script into a multi-column storyboard.
3.  Suggest B-roll shots (macro shots of circuit boards, screen recordings of Figma, etc.).

"In order to scale content, you always need to build great systems," she notes. By having AI execute the "first pass" of her storyboarding, she can focus entirely on the final 10%—the artistry that makes her work unique.

---

## The Takeaway: Differentiation in a Fragmented Culture
Kova’s success proves that AI isn't a tool for cutting corners; it’s a tool for expanding the boundaries of what a single creator can achieve. While many fear that AI will lead to a "sea of sameness," Kova argues the opposite. 

"Creative culture has gotten so much more fragmented," she says. "The creators of the future need to think about differentiation. For me, that’s the artistry that AI helps me achieve."

The barrier to entry has never been lower, but the ceiling for quality has never been higher. Kova’s final advice to the aspiring alchemists of the digital age? **"There is no better time than now to make art. Quit listening to us and go do it."**

# The Long Resurrection: How "Merchant Scum" and Lost Libraries Invented the Modern World

We are often told a tidy, tragic story about the death of ancient knowledge: a Great Library in Alexandria burns, a torch is extinguished, and the world plunges into a thousand years of darkness. 

But according to Ada Palmer—a Renaissance historian at the University of Chicago, novelist, and composer—the truth is far more complex and far more interesting. The loss of the ancient world wasn’t a single cinematic fire; it was a slow, agonizing dissolution of infrastructure. And the "Renaissance" that followed wasn’t just a lucky discovery of dusty books; it was a deliberate, 200-year-long engineering project designed to fix a broken political system that ended up, almost by accident, inventing modern science.

In a wide-ranging conversation with Dwarkesh Patel, Palmer dismantles our myths about the Renaissance, revealing a world of "merchant scum," naked dukes, and the surprisingly long road from reading Cicero to curing the plague.

### The Geography of Independence

To understand why the Renaissance happened in Italy, you have to look at the mud. When the Roman Empire dissolved in the West, the centralized state vanished. The roads became infested with bandits; the supply routes for grain and oil snapped. 

"Individual cities then needed to self-govern," Palmer explains. In most of Europe, towns emptied out. People fled to the protection of local lords who had fortified villas and private bodyguards. This created a monarchal, feudal structure. But Italy was blessed with exceptional agricultural land. Larger, wealthier Italian towns could support themselves on local resources. They didn’t need a lord; they needed a committee.

These towns looked at the ruins in their backyards and decided to revive the Roman model. They formed councils and senates. They became republics—not out of a modern love for democracy, but out of a desperate need for survival.

### Petrarch and the "Age of Ash"

By the 14th century, however, these republics were failing. Italy was wracked by civil war, mercenary raids, and the trauma of the Black Death. This is the world of Petrarch, the scholar often cited as the father of Humanism. 

Petrarch’s grief was personal. He lived through the plague, losing almost everyone he knew. When he finally received word that two young friends had survived, his joy was short-lived: one was murdered by bandits on the way to visit him, and the other was left wounded and lost in the mountains. 

"Petrarch looks around him and says, 'This is an age of ash and shadow,'" says Palmer. He concluded that the problem was a crisis of leadership. The rulers of his day—men like the fictional Lords Montague and Capulet—cared only for family honor and wealth, not the state. 

Petrarch’s solution was a radical educational experiment. He believed that if you raised a prince on the same books that produced the great Roman leaders—Plato, Homer, Cicero, Livy—the prince would, by a kind of "moral osmosis," become a virtuous leader. He wanted to recreate the educational environment of antiquity to produce a new Brutus—a man so devoted to the state that he would execute his own sons for treason.

### The "Merchant Scum" and the Power of Aesthetics

This idealistic project was quickly co-opted by the "new money" of the era: the Medici. 

In the eyes of the European aristocracy, the Medici were "merchant scum." They were bankers—usurers, in the eyes of the Church—who had no noble blood and no legitimate right to rule. Furthermore, Florence was viewed as a "pit of scum and villainy," famously known as the sodomy capital of Europe. (In France, you could be indicted for sodomy simply for having visited Florence.)

The Medici used the Renaissance as a massive public relations campaign. Imagine a French ambassador, a high-born nobleman, arriving in Florence. He expects filth. Instead, he sees a cathedral with a dome larger than anything built in a thousand years. He enters a banker’s house and finds a ten-year-old boy reciting Greek poetry about the soul. He sees bronze statues so lifelike they seem to breathe.

"The power dynamic just flipped upside down," Palmer says. "Suddenly, the condescending nobleman is in awe of the merchant scum." By dressing themselves in the trappings of Augustus and Caesar, the Medici bought legitimacy that blood alone couldn't provide. They weren't just bankers anymore; they were the architects of a new Golden Age.

### The Machiavellian Pivot

But did the "osmosis" work? Did reading Plato make better people? 

"The first generations raised by this are supposed to be philosopher princes," Palmer notes. "Instead, we get Cesare and Lucrezia Borgia." Cesare Borgia was raised on Cicero and Virgil, and he proceeded to set half the world on fire. 

The man who watched this failure most closely was Niccolò Machiavelli. He loved the classics—he famously wrote about dressing in his finest court robes just to enter his library—but he saw that virtuous leaders like Guidobaldo da Montefeltro were being crushed by monsters like the Borgias.

Machiavelli proposed a second stage for the Renaissance. If reading for virtue didn't work, perhaps they should read for *data*. He began using history as a "casebook of examples." If five battles happened next to rivers, he analyzed the decisions made in each to see what worked. He moved the study of the past from the realm of moral philosophy to the realm of political science. 

### The 200-Year Gestation of Science

The jump from Machiavelli’s political science to Galileo’s physical science seems intuitive, but Palmer emphasizes the "topsoil" required for such a leap. 

When the ancient materialist text *De Rerum Natura* by Lucretius was rediscovered in 1417, only a handful of people in the world could even read the Latin. It took 160 years of "building the libraries" before its ideas—like the existence of atoms—could spark a revolution. 

By the late 1500s, the "micro-technologies" of the book had been invented: footnotes, glossaries, and dictionaries. Printing had driven the price down. Now, a medical student in a small town could read Lucretius. They could compare his theories to their own observations. 

"They're the generation that discovers that the heart is a pump," Palmer says. "They're the generation that takes seriously the question, 'Maybe there are atoms, and maybe that's how diseases work.'" It wasn't the discovery of the book that changed the world; it was the 200-year process of creating an ecosystem where that book could be analyzed, doubted, and tested.

### The "Nine Guys in a Tower" and the Strength of Resistance

One of the most surprising insights Palmer offers is into the Florentine government itself. To prevent tyranny, Florence used a system called *sortition*—government by lottery. 

Every few months, names were pulled out of a bag. Nine random guild members (the "popolo") were chosen to rule. These men were literally locked in a tower for the duration of their term to prevent bribery or kidnapping. They had to reach a consensus to pass any law. 

While the Medici eventually learned to "rig the bag" through regulatory capture and patronage, the republican spirit remained a powerful check on their power. Palmer contrasts the Medici with the Dukes of Ferrara, who were so secure in their tyranny that they would kidnap musicians or murder servants for sport. 

The Medici, by contrast, had to be careful. They had to pretend to be mere citizens. They had to respect property rights. When the Duke wanted to knock the top off an old family’s tower to build a private corridor, the family said "no"—and the Duke listened. 

"It's a great example of how even when resistance loses, resistance wins," Palmer says. The fact that the Florentines fought so hard for their republic meant that even when they became a duchy, they enjoyed more liberties than their neighbors.

### Unintended Consequences: A Lesson for AI

The Renaissance historians of the 1400s had a plan. They wanted to create a world of virtuous, Christian, neo-Roman oligarchs. 

They failed. Instead, they created a world of democracy, secular science, and vaccines. 

"If you showed Petrarch this future, it would be scary," Palmer says. "He would be horrified by democracy... but he would see the wonders we've created, especially the fact that we can treat the Black Death, and he would weep for joy."

As we stand on the precipice of our own technological revolution with AI, Palmer’s history offers a sobering but hopeful perspective. We cannot control the "values" of the future. We cannot ensure that our tools will be used exactly as we intend. But if we focus on building the "topsoil"—the libraries, the networks of information, and the traditions of resistance—we might just create a world that, while unrecognizable to us, is capable of curing the "plagues" of the future. 

The Library of Alexandria didn't burn in a day, and the modern world wasn't built in one. It was a long, messy process of reading old books in new ways.

# Beyond the Filing Cabinet: Why the "SaaS Apocalypse" is Actually a Rebirth

For sixty years, the history of software was essentially the history of the filing cabinet. From the first IBM mainframe reservation systems in 1960 to the cloud-based CRM revolutions of the 2010s, the core mission of technology was simple: take a physical folder, turn it into a digital record, and stick it in a database.

But according to Atlassian CEO Mike Cannon-Brookes and a16z General Partner Alex Rampell, that era ended in 2022. 

As the tech world grapples with what some call the "SaaS Apocalypse"—a period of intense market volatility and fear that AI will render traditional software obsolete—the reality on the ground is more nuanced. We aren’t witnessing the death of software; we are witnessing the moment the filing cabinet finally learned how to do the work itself.

## The Locksmith Paradox: Why We Pay for Incompetence

To understand why SaaS is in a state of upheaval, you first have to understand the "Predictably Irrational" nature of how humans value work. Alex Rampell points to an anecdote from behavioral economist Dan Arieli: Imagine you’re locked out of your apartment at midnight. A locksmith arrives, picks the lock in 30 seconds, and charges you $500. You’re furious. You feel cheated by his efficiency. 

Now, imagine a second locksmith who spends nine hours drilling, sweating, and breaking tools before finally letting you in at dawn. You’re so grateful for his "hard work" that you give him a $200 tip. 

"Humans are willing to pay for incompetence," Rampell notes. "A lot of pricing is about fairness." 

For a decade, SaaS companies used "per-seat" pricing because it felt fair. If a company had 500 employees using a tool, they paid more than a company with ten. But AI destroys this logic. If an AI agent can do the work of 50 customer service reps in seconds, the "per-seat" model collapses. This is the heart of the market’s fear: if the "effort" (the seats) disappears, does the revenue disappear too?

## The Three Flavors of SaaS Survival

Rampell argues that the public markets are currently panicking because they can’t distinguish between three very different types of software companies:

### 1. The Endangered: Seats Tied to Outcomes
In this category, exemplified by companies like Zendesk, seats are occupied by people performing a specific task (answering tickets). If an AI can answer those tickets, the need for seats drops to zero. Unless these companies pivot to "outcome-based" pricing, their revenue streams are in peril.

### 2. The Safe: The System of Record
Then there are companies like Workday or QuickBooks. A giant corporation like GE doesn't pay for Workday because 300,000 employees are "producing an outcome" inside the app every day. They pay per employee because it’s a "fair" way to tax a large enterprise for maintaining its vital records. "Nobody is going to get rid of QuickBooks," Rampell says. In fact, these companies win because they own the data that AI needs to be useful.

### 3. The Hybrid: Creative Extensibility
Companies like Adobe or Atlassian sit in the middle. While AI might make a designer or a developer faster, the goal isn't to have *fewer* designs or *less* code—it’s to produce more. In these "output-constrained" fields, AI acts as an accelerator rather than a replacement.

## From "Systems of Record" to "Systems of Process"

While the industry loves the term "System of Record," Mike Cannon-Brookes finds it reductive. "It sounds like a database sitting there, very static," he says. "To me, businesses are a set of processes, not a system of record."

Cannon-Brookes argues that a modern business—especially a knowledge-based one—is simply a collection of coordinated processes. Some of these are **input-constrained**: your legal team doesn't want to "generate" more work; they just want to process the existing NDAs and contracts as efficiently as possible. 

Others are **output-constrained**: your software engineers and marketers have an unlimited ceiling for the value they can create. 

The "SaaS Apocalypse" narrative ignores the fact that AI allows software to move from being a passive container for these processes to an active participant. In the 1960s, the "Saber" reservation system (built by IBM and American Airlines) replaced vaults of filing cabinets with a SQL database. It was a miracle of organization, but the database couldn't "think." 

"Now," Cannon-Brookes says, "the filing cabinet can do work. QuickBooks can actually accomplish a task by itself versus just relying on a human to retrieve the file."

## The "Vibe Coding" Delusion

One of the most buzzed-about concepts in the AI era is "vibe coding"—the idea that non-programmers can simply "vibe" an application into existence by describing it to an AI. While this is a breakthrough for personal productivity, both Rampell and Cannon-Brookes are skeptical of it replacing enterprise software.

"The idea that I would vibe code my own Workday and then run it is terrifying," Cannon-Brookes laughs. He cites the "Indiana Problem": a piece of HR software isn't just a database; it’s a repository of thousands of edge cases, like what to do when an employee in Indiana goes on maternity leave under a specific state law.

Software is an accumulation of "deterministic rules learned from decades of experience." You can’t vibe-code experience. However, Cannon-Brookes sees a massive future for **extensibility**. Instead of building a whole new HR system, a manager might "vibe code" a custom interface that sits on top of Atlassian or Workday to solve a hyper-specific problem for their local team. 

## The Casino Chip Problem: Why Consumption Pricing Isn't the Answer

If "per-seat" pricing is dying, is "consumption pricing" (paying per AI token or per task) the savior? Not necessarily. 

Cannon-Brookes warns that customers often loathe consumption models because they feel like "casino chips." If a vendor adds a new AI feature that suddenly summarizes every document, and the customer’s bill spikes 10x overnight without their consent, the relationship sours. 

"Customers want predictability," he says. "They’ve been burned by consumption-based billing where the bill just goes up and they don’t know how to control it." 

The future likely belongs to a hybrid model: a predictable base fee for the "system of record" and a value-based fee for the "AI agents" that actually perform the labor.

## The Verdict: Execution over Apocalypse

The "SaaS Apocalypse" is less of an ending and more of a Great Filter. The companies that treat software as a static filing cabinet will likely be disrupted by AI agents that can do the filing themselves. 

But for the "Systems of Process"—the tools that coordinate human creativity and manage the complex edge cases of global business—this is an era of unprecedented expansion. 

"This is the best thing that's happened to our business," Cannon-Brookes concludes. "We have tools to play with knowledge, to act on it, and to solve the jobs customers have always hired us for. Logically, this is very good. But it’s up to us to execute through that transition."

인터뷰의 후반부에서 샘 킨은 제2차 세계대전 당시 나치의 원자폭탄 개발을 막기 위한 비밀 미션, **'알소스(Alsos) 작전'**에 대해 이야기합니다.

*   **공포의 근원:** 독일의 천재 과학자 베르너 하이젠베르크(Werner Heisenberg)가 원자폭탄을 만들고 있다는 소문은 연합군을 공포에 떨게 했습니다. 하이젠베르크의 실험실에서 발생한 단순한 화학 폭발이 '핵 연쇄 반응 성공'으로 와전되면서, 미국은 맨해튼 프로젝트에 박차를 가함과 동시에 독일의 연구를 방해하기 위한 특수 부대를 조직했습니다.

*   **중수소(Heavy Water) 파괴와 우라늄 탈취:** 노르웨이의 중수소 공장을 파괴하고, 나치가 확보한 우라늄 원석 수천 톤을 가로챈 알소스 작전의 성과는 결국 나치의 핵 개발을 좌절시켰습니다. 역설적이게도 이때 가로챈 우라늄 중 일부는 이듬해 히로시마에 투하된 원자폭탄의 재료가 되었습니다.

### 결론: 과거는 살아있는 미래다

샘 킨이 전하는 실험 고고학의 세계는 우리에게 중요한 메시지를 던집니다. 과거의 인류는 미개했던 것이 아니라, 그들만의 방식으로 최선의 최첨단을 달리고 있었다는 점입니다. 

역사는 단순히 책 속에 박제된 기록이 아닙니다. 우리가 직접 재현하고, 맛보고, 경험할 때 과거는 비로소 살아 움직이며 현대의 문제를 해결할 영감을 제공합니다. 잃어버린 기술(Lost Technologies)은 사라진 것이 아니라, 우리가 다시 발견해주기를 기다리고 있는 인류의 거대한 데이터베이스인 것입니다.

## [인사이트] 워런 버핏은 ‘자본’을 배분했고, 미래의 승자는 ‘지능’을 배분한다

**하버드 경영대학원 렘 코닝 교수가 말하는 AI 네이티브 기업의 생존 전략**

과거의 비즈니스 세계에서 승자는 자원을 가장 효율적으로 배분하는 사람이었습니다. 워런 버핏(Warren Buffett)은 자본을 어디에 투자할지 누구보다 잘 결정하며 버크셔 해서웨이를 키웠고, 맥킨지(McKinsey) 같은 컨설팅 펌은 최고의 인재를 적재적소에 배치하는 능력으로 시장을 장악했습니다. 

하지만 지금 우리는 새로운 시대에 진입하고 있습니다. 하버드 경영대학원(HBS)에서 기업가 정신과 AI를 연구하는 렘 코닝(Rem Koning) 교수는 이제 시장의 패권이 **‘지능의 배분(Intelligence Allocation)’** 능력을 갖춘 자들에게 넘어갈 것이라고 단언합니다. 어떤 업무를 인간이 하고, 어떤 업무를 어떤 AI 모델(GPT, Claude, DeepSeek 등)에게 맡길지 결정하는 능력이 기업의 핵심 경쟁력이 된다는 것입니다.

---

### 1. AI는 평등한 도구인가, 격차를 벌리는 증폭기인가?

흔히 AI가 정보의 격차를 줄여주는 ‘평등 장치(Equalizer)’라고 생각하기 쉽습니다. 누구나 AI를 통해 코딩을 하고, 전문적인 보고서를 쓰고, 디자인을 할 수 있게 되었기 때문입니다. 하지만 코닝 교수의 연구 결과는 사뭇 다릅니다. AI는 오히려 실력 있는 자들의 능력을 극대화하는 **‘증폭기(Amplifier)’**에 가깝습니다.

코닝 교수가 3년 전 케냐의 소상공인들을 대상으로 진행한 연구는 충격적인 시사점을 던져줍니다. 연구팀은 왓츠앱(WhatsApp)을 통해 기업가들에게 AI 조언 서비스를 제공했습니다. 결과는 양극화되었습니다.

*   **하위 그룹:** 기존에 성과가 낮았던 사업가들은 AI와 대화한 후 오히려 수익이 10% 감소했습니다. AI가 주는 수많은 조언 중 무엇이 좋은 조언이고 무엇이 나쁜 조언인지 구분할 수 있는 **‘판단력(Judgment)’**이 부족했기 때문입니다. 결국 AI가 만든 그럴듯한 쓰레기 정보(Slop)에 휘둘려 잘못된 결정을 내린 것입니다.
*   **상위 그룹:** 반면 이미 평균 이상의 성과를 내던 사업가들은 AI를 통해 수익을 더욱 끌어올렸습니다. 이들은 AI의 답변을 비판적으로 수용하고, 자신의 사업 맥락에 맞는 최적의 해답을 골라내는 능력이 있었습니다.

결국 AI 시대에 가장 중요한 자산은 기술 그 자체가 아니라, AI의 결과물을 걸러내고 방향을 잡는 인간의 **‘숙련된 판단력’**과 **‘직관’**입니다.

---

### 2. 'AI를 쓰는 기업'과 'AI 네이티브 기업'의 결정적 차이

코닝 교수는 단순히 업무 효율을 위해 AI를 사용하는 수준을 넘어, 처음부터 AI를 중심에 두고 설계된 **‘AI 네이티브(AI-Native)’** 기업으로 진화해야 한다고 강조합니다.

그 차이는 '프로세스'와 '제품'의 결합 방식에서 나타납니다.
*   **AI 활용 기업:** 직원이 코딩을 하거나 고객 응대 이메일을 쓸 때 AI의 도움을 받습니다. 이는 업무 속도를 높여주지만, 여전히 사람이 개입해야 하므로 확장에 한계가 있습니다.
*   **AI 네이티브 기업 (예: Gamma):** AI가 제품 그 자체에 녹아들어 있습니다. 사용자가 몇 줄의 문장만 입력하면 AI가 직접 프레젠테이션 덱을 만들어냅니다. 사람이 중간에서 수작업을 할 필요가 없으므로, 수만 명의 디자이너를 고용하는 대신 **클라우드 컴퓨팅 파워(Compute)**만 늘리면 무한히 확장할 수 있습니다.

이처럼 **'인건비(Headcount)'가 아닌 '연산 비용(Compute)'에 비례해 성장하는 경제 구조**를 만드는 것이 AI 네이티브 전략의 핵심입니다.

---

### 3. 챗봇의 시대는 가고, 에이전트(Agent)의 시대가 온다

우리는 지난 2년 동안 챗봇(Chatbot)과 대화하는 방식에 익숙해졌습니다. 하지만 코닝 교수는 "우리에겐 더 이상의 챗봇이 필요하지 않다"고 말합니다. 이제는 단순히 조언을 해주는 수준을 넘어, 실제로 행동하고 결과물을 만들어내는 **‘에이전틱 AI(Agentic AI)’**가 비즈니스의 병목 현상을 해결할 것입니다.

예를 들어, 마케팅 전략이 고민인 창업가에게 "이런 전략을 써보세요"라고 말하는 챗봇은 큰 도움이 되지 않습니다. 대신, 직접 마케팅 플랜을 짜고 광고 소재를 제작해 실행까지 완료하는 AI 에이전트가 필요합니다. 이는 인력과 자본이 부족한 개발도상국의 창업가나 소규모 스타트업에게 뉴욕이나 실리콘밸리의 전문가를 고용한 것과 다름없는 파급력을 제공할 것입니다.

---

### 4. '바이브 코딩(Vibe Coding)'과 소프트웨어의 민주화

최근 등장한 ‘바이브 코딩(Vibe Coding, 자연어 대화만으로 소프트웨어를 개발하는 방식)’은 경제 구조를 근본적으로 바꿀 잠재력이 있습니다. 과거에는 막대한 벤처 캐피털(VC) 투자를 받아 개발자를 대거 고용해야 소프트웨어를 만들 수 있었지만, 이제는 창업가 혼자서도 정교한 서비스를 구축할 수 있습니다.

이는 전 세계적인 **'소프트웨어 경제화'**를 불러올 것입니다. 
*   태국에서 식당을 운영하는 주인이 자신에게 딱 맞는 맞춤형 고객 관리(CRM) 시스템을 AI로 직접 만들어 사용합니다. 
*   인도의 금융 결제 인프라(UPI)가 미국을 앞질렀듯, 신흥 시장의 창업가들이 기존의 제약을 뛰어넘어 AI를 통해 비즈니스를 혁신하는 '리프프로깅(Leapfrogging, 단계를 뛰어넘는 발전)' 현상이 가속화될 것입니다.

---

### 5. 창업가를 위한 조언: "AI 공학보다 '도메인 통찰력'에 집중하라"

AI 기술이 상향 평준화되면서 역설적으로 가장 중요해진 것은 **‘문제 정의 능력’**입니다. 코닝 교수는 창업가들이 범하기 쉬운 가장 위험한 가정이 "AI로 만들었으니 사람들이 좋아할 것"이라는 생각이라고 경고합니다.

*   **과잉 엔지니어링 주의:** AI 도구가 너무 재미있어서 사용자가 원하지도 않는 기능을 붙이느라 한 달을 허비하지 마세요.
*   **핵심 통찰(Earned Insight):** 최신 AI 모델을 확보하는 것보다 중요한 것은, 특정 분야의 문제를 누구보다 깊게 이해하고 AI를 어디에 '한 방울' 떨어뜨려 가치를 만들지 찾아내는 것입니다.
*   **제어권의 역전:** 앤스로픽(Anthropic)의 에듀케이션 리더 드류 벤트(Drew Bent)는 미래에 AI가 고도의 전략적 사고를 담당하고, 인간은 AI가 내린 결정에 '취향'과 '주관'을 더해 최종 승인을 하는 '제어권의 역전' 현상이 일어날 수 있다고 예측합니다.

### 결론: 지능을 배분하는 전략가가 되어라

AI는 모든 것을 해결해 주는 마법 지팡이가 아닙니다. 오히려 준비되지 않은 자에게는 잘못된 길을 안내하는 위험한 나침반이 될 수 있습니다. 

미래의 승자는 AI를 단순히 사용하는 사람이 아닙니다. **인간의 고유한 판단력과 AI의 무한한 지능을 어떻게 섞고(Blend), 조율하고(Orchestrate), 배분(Allocate)할지 결정하는 전략가**입니다. 당신은 오늘 당신의 지능을 어떻게 배분하고 있습니까? AI에게 어떤 복잡한 숙제를 던지고 있습니까? 이제 우리의 야망을 AI의 수준만큼 높여야 할 때입니다.

### 1. GDP라는 숫자가 놓치고 있는 것: '소비자 잉여'의 마법

모키르 교수는 경제 성장을 단순히 1인당 GDP(국내총생산)의 수치로만 판단하는 것에 회의적입니다. GDP는 경제의 구조가 크게 변하지 않는 단기적인 변동을 측정하기엔 유용하지만, 수백 년에 걸친 인류의 복지 변화를 설명하기엔 턱없이 부족하기 때문입니다.

그는 이를 설명하기 위해 **'소비자 잉여(Consumer Surplus)'**라는 개념을 강조합니다. 소비자 잉여란 소비자가 어떤 상품이나 서비스를 이용하기 위해 기꺼이 지불할 용의가 있는 금액과 실제 지불한 금액 사이의 차이를 말합니다.

*   **마취 없는 수술의 공포:** 19세기 중반 이전의 모든 수술은 마취 없이 진행되었습니다. 환자는 고통을 참기 위해 총알을 깨물어야 했고, 의사는 쇼크사를 막기 위해 2분 안에 수술을 끝내야 했습니다. 오늘날 우리에게 "얼마를 주면 마취 없이 맹장 수술을 받겠느냐"고 묻는다면, 그 누구도 응하지 않을 것입니다. 마취 기술은 인류에게 엄청난 가치를 선사했지만, GDP 수치에는 거의 나타나지 않습니다.
*   **공짜가 된 기술들:** 오늘날 우리가 무료로 사용하는 GPS, 화상 통화, 디지털 사진 등은 과거에는 상상할 수 없을 만큼 비싸거나 존재하지 않았던 것들입니다. 하지만 '가격이 0원'인 서비스는 GDP에 집계되지 않습니다. 즉, 우리는 GDP 수치가 보여주는 것보다 훨씬 더 풍요로운 세상에 살고 있는 셈입니다.

### 2. 진보를 이끄는 '상위 3%'의 힘과 문화의 역할

모키르 교수의 주장 중 가장 흥미로우면서도 논쟁적인 대목은 **"진보는 인구의 아주 일부분에 의해 주도된다"**는 점입니다. 그는 노동 인구의 약 2~3%에 해당하는 '상위 꼬리(Upper Tail)'의 인재들이 문화를 근본적으로 변화시키고 기술 혁신을 이끈다고 주장합니다.

여기서 말하는 문화란 단순히 예술이나 관습이 아니라, **'자연계를 바라보는 태도'**와 **'유용한 지식(Useful Knowledge)을 축적하고 전파하려는 의지'**를 의미합니다.

*   **제도보다 앞서는 문화:** 많은 경제학자(대런 애쓰모글루 등)들이 '제도(Institutions)'가 성장의 핵심이라고 말할 때, 모키르 교수는 **"제도는 문화에서 비롯된다"**고 역설합니다. 새로운 아이디어가 싹틀 수 있는 토양, 즉 실패를 용인하고 지적 호기심을 장려하는 문화가 먼저 존재해야 비로소 혁신적인 제도가 안착할 수 있다는 논리입니다.
*   **이스라엘의 사례:** 모키르 교수는 이스라엘의 하이텍 산업 성공 비결로 '실패해도 괜찮은 문화'와 '박스 밖이 아니라 아예 박스가 없다고 생각하는 사고방식'을 꼽습니다. 또한, 교육과 인적 자본을 중시하는 이민자들의 문화적 배경이 혁신의 원동력이 되었다고 분석합니다.

### 3. 역사의 '하키 스틱' 곡선은 어떻게 만들어졌나?

인류 역사의 대부분 기간 동안 경제 성장 곡선은 평탄했습니다. 그러다 18세기 후반 산업혁명을 기점으로 급격히 솟구치는 '하키 스틱' 모양을 그리게 됩니다. 모키르 교수는 이를 세 가지 핵심 기술의 수렴으로 설명합니다.

1.  **전기(Electricity):** 존재는 알았지만 활용법을 몰랐던 전기를 1860~70년대에 저렴하게 생산하는 법을 터득하면서 가전, 조명, 난방의 혁명이 시작되었습니다.
2.  **강철(Steel):** 베세머 공법(Bessemer process)을 통해 양질의 강철을 대량 생산하게 되면서 거대 선박과 철도가 등장했고, 이는 물류비용의 획기적인 하락으로 이어졌습니다.
3.  **화학(Chemicals):** 유기화학의 발전은 비료, 염료, 의약품의 혁신을 가져왔습니다.

이러한 기술들은 단순히 개별적으로 존재한 것이 아니라, 프랜시스 베이컨이 주창한 **'과학을 기술에 적용하여 인류를 부유하게 만든다'**는 철학 아래 서로 상호작용하며 폭발적인 시너지를 냈습니다.

---

### 4. 현대 사회가 직면한 'asteroid(소행성)'와 미래에 대한 우려

낙관주의적 견해를 유지해온 모키르 교수지만, 21세기에 들어서며 그의 낙관론에도 약간의 그림자가 드리워졌습니다. 그는 인류가 직면한 위협을 세 가지로 요약합니다.

*   **기후 변화:** 이는 실존적인 위협입니다. 기술적 해결책(탄소 포집 등)이 나오고는 있지만, 기후 변화로 인한 국제 관계의 혼란과 갈등은 기술만으로 해결하기 어려운 문제입니다.
*   **AI(인공지능)의 양날의 검:** AI는 인쇄기 발명 이후 가장 큰 혁명이 될 것이며, 특히 개인 맞춤형 교육과 정밀 의료 분야에서 놀라운 진보를 가져올 것입니다. 하지만 동시에 악용될 소지도 다분합니다.
*   **핵무기와 제도적 퇴행(Institutional Deterioration):** 모키르 교수가 가장 밤잠을 설치게 하는 위협은 핵무기 확산과 정치적 리더들의 질적 저하입니다. 기술은 누적되어 계속 발전하지만, 제도는 언제든 퇴보할 수 있기 때문입니다. 그는 "우리의 능력(기술)은 현대적인 소프트웨어를 실행하고 있지만, 우리의 하드웨어(인간의 본성과 권력 구조)는 여전히 구식의 경쟁적인 상태에 머물러 있다"고 지적합니다.

---

### 5. 우리가 나아가야 할 길: 개방과 협력

기사 말미에서 모키르 교수는 현재 서구 사회가 겪고 있는 **이민에 대한 적대적 태도**를 '역사상 가장 뼈아픈 실책(unforced error)'이라고 강하게 비판합니다.

*   **이민자는 혁신의 원천:** 통계에 따르면 이민자들은 현지인보다 더 많은 특허를 출원하며, 그 특허의 질 또한 두 배 이상 높습니다. 그들은 일자리를 뺏는 존재가 아니라 새로운 가치를 창출하고 세금을 내며 사회 시스템을 지탱하는 동력입니다.
*   **규칙 기반의 경쟁:** 성장에 도움이 되는 경쟁은 반드시 규칙(Rules) 안에서 이루어져야 합니다. 하지만 최근 국제 사회에서 이러한 규칙들이 무시되고 파괴되는 '제도적 퇴행'이 나타나고 있다는 점이 큰 우려 사항입니다.

### 결론: 진보는 당연한 것이 아니다

조엘 모키르 교수의 메시지는 명확합니다. 인류가 누리는 현재의 풍요는 결코 우연이나 당연한 결과가 아닙니다. 그것은 지식을 갈구하고, 실패를 두려워하지 않으며, 과학을 삶에 적용하려 했던 소수의 혁신가와 그들을 뒷받침한 문화적 토양이 만들어낸 기적입니다.

우리가 이 진보를 이어가기 위해서는 기술적 혁신만큼이나 **'제도적 건전성'**과 **'개방적인 문화'**를 지키는 데 힘써야 합니다. "과거는 나빴지만, 미래는 우리가 어떻게 하느냐에 따라 더 나빠질 수도, 혹은 상상도 못 할 만큼 좋아질 수도 있다"는 그의 경고와 희망은 우리에게 시사하는 바가 큽니다.

## [심층 분석] 르네상스라는 거대한 실험: 고전 읽기가 어떻게 현대 과학을 탄생시켰는가

**역사가 에이다 팔머(Ada Palmer)가 들려주는 지식의 복원과 권력의 심리학**

우리는 흔히 르네상스를 '암흑기 이후 예술과 학문이 화려하게 부활한 시대'로 기억합니다. 하지만 시카고 대학교의 역사가이자 소설가인 에이다 팔머(Ada Palmer)는 르네상스를 조금 다른 관점에서 바라봅니다. 그녀에게 르네상스는 인류를 구원하려 했던 한 지식인의 '실패한 교육 실험'이었으며, 그 실패의 잔해 위에서 현대 과학과 정치학이 우연히 싹튼 역설적인 과정이었습니다.

### 1. 로마의 멸망과 이탈리아 도시 공화국의 탄생

서로마 제국이 붕괴했을 때, 유럽 전역의 도시들은 각자도생의 길을 걸어야 했습니다. 중앙 정부가 사라지자 보급로가 끊기고 도로는 도적 떼의 소굴이 되었습니다. 이 과정에서 도시들은 두 가지 선택지에 직면했습니다.

*   **군주제 혹은 마을(Village)화:** 자급자족 능력이 부족한 작은 마을들은 지역 유력자(귀족)의 보호 아래로 들어갔습니다. 사람들은 안전을 담보로 자유를 포기했고, 이는 봉건적 군주제의 기초가 되었습니다.
*   **도시 공화국(Republic):** 반면 이탈리아는 비옥한 농지와 풍부한 자원을 바탕으로 도시 자체가 스스로를 방어하고 통치할 경제력을 갖추고 있었습니다. 베네치아, 피렌체, 제노바 같은 도시들이 고대 로마의 원로원 모델을 본떠 공화정 체제를 유지할 수 있었던 이유입니다.

### 2. 페트라르카의 원대한 꿈: "공부하면 성군이 된다"

14세기, 흑사병과 끊임없는 내전으로 피폐해진 이탈리아를 보며 인문주의의 아버지 페트라르카(Petrarch)는 한 가지 결론에 도달합니다. **"우리 시대가 이토록 처참한 이유는 지도자들이 이기적이기 때문이다."**

그는 고대 로마의 지도자들이 가졌던 덕성(Virtue)을 복원하고자 했습니다. 국가를 위해 아들까지 처형했던 브루투스(Brutus) 같은 인물을 키워내기 위해, 그는 '교육의 삼투압 현상(Osmosis)'을 믿었습니다. 즉, 젊은 왕자들이 키케로, 플라톤, 호메로스를 읽으며 자란다면 자연스럽게 고대의 고결한 성품을 닮게 될 것이라는 믿음이었습니다.

이 '페트라르카 프로젝트'를 위해 유럽의 지식인들은 알프스를 넘고 콘스탄티노플까지 가서 잊혔던 고대 필사본들을 찾아 나섰습니다. 도서관을 짓고, 그리스어를 가르치며, 다음 세대의 지도자들을 고전의 바다에 빠뜨렸습니다.

### 3. '상인 쓰레기'에서 '위대한 군주'로: 문화라는 프로파간다

하지만 이 숭고한 교육 열풍 뒤에는 현실적인 권력의 계산이 깔려 있었습니다. 당시 피렌체의 메디치(Medici) 가문은 유럽 귀족들의 눈에 그저 '돈만 많은 상인 쓰레기(Merchant scum)'에 불과했습니다. 정통성도 없고 가문 배경도 미천했던 그들에게 고전 문화는 완벽한 신분 세탁의 도구였습니다.

프랑스 대사나 교황청 인사가 피렌체를 방문했을 때, 그들은 압도적인 광경을 목격합니다.
*   살아 움직이는 듯한 정교한 청동상(고대 로마의 부활).
*   이전 시대에는 상상도 못 했던 거대한 돔(두오모).
*   10살짜리 꼬마가 고대 그리스어로 플라톤의 철학 시를 낭송하는 모습.

메디치 가문은 고전 문화를 통해 **"우리는 단순한 장사꾼이 아니라, 로마 제국의 영광을 재현하는 카이사르의 후예다"**라는 메시지를 전 세계에 발신했습니다. 이는 강력한 외교적 무기가 되어, 무시당하던 상인 가문이 유럽의 강대국들과 대등한 동맹을 맺게 하는 원동력이 되었습니다.

### 4. 마키아벨리의 깨달음: 교육 실험의 실패와 정치학의 탄생

시간이 흘러 고전 교육을 받은 세대가 권력을 잡았습니다. 결과는 어땠을까요? 페트라르카의 기대와 달리, 체사레 보르자(Cesare Borgia) 같은 지도자들은 라틴어와 그리스어에 능통했지만 동시에 그 누구보다 잔혹하고 기만적이었습니다.

이 과정을 지켜본 인물이 바로 니콜로 마키아벨리(Niccolò Machiavelli)입니다. 그는 깨달았습니다. **"고전을 읽는다고 해서 반드시 선한 사람이 되는 것은 아니다."**

여기서 마키아벨리는 중요한 전환을 시도합니다. 그는 역사를 '도덕 책'이 아닌 **'데이터베이스(Casebook)'**로 보기 시작했습니다. 
*   "과거 강가에서 치러진 5번의 전투 사례를 분석해 보자."
*   "어떤 결정이 승리를 이끌었고, 어떤 결정이 패배를 불렀는가?"

이것이 현대 **정치학(Political Science)**의 시초입니다. 도덕적 감화가 아닌, 객관적인 사례 분석을 통해 최선의 선택을 도출하는 방식입니다.

### 5. 도서관에서 실험실로: 과학 혁명의 숨은 동력

에이다 팔머는 르네상스 도서관의 확충이 어떻게 **과학 혁명(Scientific Revolution)**으로 이어졌는지 상세히 설명합니다.

1.  **정보의 인프라 구축:** 인쇄술의 발명과 함께 각주(Footnote), 색인, 용어 사전 같은 '미세 기술'들이 발달했습니다.
2.  **지식의 대중화:** 과거에는 전 세계에서 단 20명만 읽을 수 있었던 루크레티우스(Lucretius)의 원자론적 텍스트가 100년 후에는 3만 명의 의대생과 법대생들에게 읽혔습니다.
3.  **가설의 검증:** 고전 텍스트를 읽던 사람들은 단순히 내용을 수용하는 것을 넘어, "이게 정말 사실일까?"라고 의심하기 시작했습니다. 그들은 자연을 마키아벨리가 역사를 분석했던 방식대로 분석하기 시작했습니다.

루크레티우스의 책이 발견된 지 160년 만에 인류는 '심장이 펌프 역할을 한다'는 사실을 알아냈고, 세균설(Germ theory)의 기초를 닦았습니다. 지식의 토양(Topsoil)이 충분히 쌓인 후에야 과학이라는 나무가 자랄 수 있었던 것입니다.

### 6. 저항의 유산: 피렌체가 남긴 민주주의의 씨앗

피렌체는 귀족 계급을 몰살하고 상인 길드원들이 추첨제(Sortition)를 통해 통치하는 독특한 '평민 공화국'이었습니다. 비록 나중에 메디치 가문이 독재를 펼치며 공화정이 무너졌지만, 시민들의 강렬한 공화주의 정신은 사라지지 않았습니다.

피렌체의 군주들은 시민들의 반발이 두려워 다른 도시의 폭군들처럼 제멋대로 행동할 수 없었습니다. 팔머는 이를 **"저항이 실패하더라도, 그 과정에서 남겨진 전통과 구조가 폭정의 수위를 낮춘다"**고 설명합니다. 이는 현대 민주주의 국가에서도 시사하는 바가 큽니다.

### 결론: 의도하지 않은 미래를 만드는 힘

페트라르카는 흑사병을 고칠 수 있는 세상을 꿈꾸지 않았습니다. 그는 단지 고대의 덕성을 가진 지도자를 원했을 뿐입니다. 하지만 그가 시작한 '도서관 짓기'와 '고전 복원'이라는 집요한 노력은 200년 후 과학 혁명이라는, 그가 상상조차 할 수 없었던 열매를 맺었습니다.

에이다 팔머의 통찰은 우리에게 묻습니다. 우리가 오늘날 AI나 새로운 기술을 통해 만들고자 하는 미래는 과연 우리의 의도대로 흘러갈까요? 역사는 말해줍니다. 우리가 심은 씨앗은 종종 우리가 상상한 것보다 훨씬 더 기이하고 위대한 숲을 이룬다는 사실을 말입니다.

**주요 용어 정리:**
*   **인문주의(Humanism):** 신 중심의 사고에서 벗어나 인간의 가치와 고대 그리스·로마의 고전을 연구하던 사상적 흐름.
*   **추첨제(Sortition):** 선거가 아닌 제비뽑기를 통해 공직자를 선출하는 방식. 르네상스 피렌체와 고대 아테네에서 사용됨.
*   **삼투압 교육론(Osmosis Theory):** 특정 환경이나 지식에 지속적으로 노출되면 자연스럽게 그 특성을 습득하게 된다는 믿음.
