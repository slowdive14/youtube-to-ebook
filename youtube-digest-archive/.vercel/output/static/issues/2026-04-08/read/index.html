From E.T. to A.I.: The Radical Shift in the Hunt for Alien Life

For generations, the search for extraterrestrial intelligence (SETI) has largely been defined by a singular, iconic image: a lone radio telescope dish scanning the vast cosmos, listening for a deliberate message from another civilization. This vision, immortalized in popular culture like the film “Contact,” represents a kind of “gold standard” – the undeniable proof of intelligent life beyond Earth. Yet, as renowned planetary scientist Sara Seager reveals, humanity’s quest for cosmic companions is undergoing a profound transformation, broadening its scope far beyond traditional radio signals to encompass entirely new and unexpected signatures of intelligence, including those left by advanced artificial intelligence.

Seager, a professor at MIT and a leading figure in exoplanet research, points to a fundamental human challenge: “It’s so hard to think outside of the box, outside of who we are.” Our imagination is often limited by our own experiences, making it difficult to conceive of alien life or intelligence that doesn’t mirror our own. This inherent bias has historically narrowed our search, but scientists are now actively working to overcome it, pushing the boundaries of what we consider a “sign of life.”

Beyond the Radio Dish: The Rise of Technosignatures

While the dream of receiving a direct, purposeful radio message remains potent, the sheer improbability of such an event is a sobering reality. The universe is unfathomably vast, and the odds of an alien civilization beaming a signal directly at our tiny planet, at the precise moment we are listening, are astronomically small. Moreover, constantly scanning the entire sky for such a fleeting signal is a monumental task.

This recognition has led to a significant paradigm shift: the growing emphasis on technosignatures. A technosignature is defined as any observable evidence of technological capability from an intelligent civilization. Unlike a direct message, these are often unintended byproducts of a society’s existence, much like the emissions and structures our own civilization produces.

Seager outlines several compelling examples of what these technosignatures might entail:

  • Atmospheric Changes: Just as industrial activity on Earth has altered our atmosphere, an advanced alien civilization might leave detectable chemical fingerprints in the atmosphere of its home planet.
  • Satellite Swarms: Imagine vast constellations of satellites, far more extensive than anything Earth currently possesses, orbiting an alien world. These swarms could be detectable as large, shiny objects or even a “patchy sphere” of reflective material around a distant star.
  • City Lights or Large Structures: The illumination from sprawling alien cities or monumental engineering projects could be visible from light-years away. Think of structures on a scale far grander than our tallest skyscrapers or most expansive metropolises.
  • Waste Heat (Dyson Spheres): One particularly intriguing technosignature involves the search for “excess heat.” A highly advanced civilization might construct megastructures, such as a Dyson sphere or swarm, to harness the entire energy output of its star. Such a structure would emit a distinctive infrared signature, indicating enormous energy consumption. Scientists have already scoured existing infrared surveys of our sky, setting limits on how many such spheres could exist undetected.

These concepts are no longer mere science fiction; they are active areas of research, with astronomers meticulously sifting through astronomical data for any anomalies that might hint at intelligent construction.

The AI Revolution: A New Lens for Alien Intelligence

Perhaps the most thought-provoking and somewhat unsettling aspect of this evolving search is the growing consideration of post-biological intelligence, particularly in the context of artificial intelligence (AI). Seager notes that living in an era where “AI accelerating in its so-called ‘intelligence’ is terrifying in a way, really,” bringing the question of advanced computer intelligence to the forefront of our minds.

This rapid development on Earth prompts a crucial question: What if advanced alien civilizations have already transitioned beyond biological life? What if their dominant form of intelligence is artificial? This isn’t just a philosophical query; it has direct implications for how we search.

If AI becomes the prevailing intelligence on an exoplanet, its “signature” might be very different from what we typically imagine for organic life. Our own planet, for instance, is increasingly surrounded by constellations of satellites for communication and Earth observation – a nascent “signature of AI” that could, in theory, be detected from afar. Extending this idea, an alien AI civilization might construct even more massive satellite arrays or other technological manifestations that don’t fit our traditional biological assumptions.

This concept delves into what some refer to as the “Great Filter” – a hypothetical barrier that life must overcome to reach advanced intelligence and spacefaring capabilities. One theory suggests that the filter might be the transition to post-biological intelligence itself, or what comes after. “This is like a frontiers research question about what AI will leave behind,” Seager explains, and “what does it mean for potential AI computer type intelligence elsewhere?”

A Scratch at the Surface

Despite the expansive list of ideas and the dedicated efforts of scientists, the universe has yet to yield definitive proof of alien intelligence. “So far they haven’t turned anything up,” Seager admits. However, she emphasizes that these sophisticated searches are still “just a scratch at the surface” of the vast cosmic ocean.

The shift in criteria for the search for aliens is a testament to human ingenuity and our capacity to adapt our understanding in the face of the unknown. By moving beyond our anthropocentric biases and embracing the possibilities of diverse forms of intelligence – from biological beings leaving technosignatures to advanced artificial intelligences – we are expanding our chances of finally answering humanity’s most profound question: Are we alone? The universe is immense, and the search has only just begun to truly think outside the box.

The Elusive Quest: How Science Truly Advances, and What It Means for the Age of AI

The story of scientific discovery is often presented as a neat, linear progression: a hypothesis is formed, an experiment is conducted, the hypothesis is either confirmed or falsified, and thus, knowledge marches forward. But as Michael Nielsen, a luminary whose diverse contributions span quantum computing, open science, and deep learning, reveals in a recent conversation with Dwarkesh Patel, the reality is far more complex, mysterious, and even elusive. This profound understanding of science’s true nature holds critical implications for how we envision and leverage artificial intelligence in accelerating future breakthroughs.

The Michelson-Morley Myth: More Than Just Falsification

Take, for instance, the famous Michelson-Morley experiment of the 1880s. The popular narrative suggests this experiment conclusively disproved the existence of the “ether”—a hypothetical medium through which light was thought to propagate—thereby creating a crisis in physics that Albert Einstein heroically resolved with his theory of special relativity. Nielsen, drawing on biographies and histories of science, dismantles this simplistic account.

In truth, Michelson and Morley weren’t trying to disprove the ether, but rather to test different competing theories about its nature. For centuries, physicists had debated the ether, much like Boyle in the 1600s, who, noting light could travel through a vacuum while sound could not, posited an invisible medium for light waves. Michelson and Morley specifically sought to detect an “ether wind”—a directional current that the Earth might pass through, subtly affecting light’s speed. Their surprising result: no ether wind was detected.

This outcome, however, didn’t immediately spell the death of the ether. It merely ruled out some theories while leaving others intact. Indeed, Michelson himself continued to believe in the ether until his death in the late 1920s, conducting experiments well into his later years. Other physicists, like Miller, even claimed to detect ether effects from high altitudes, prompting Einstein’s famous, “Subtle is the Lord, but malicious He is not.”

Crucially, Einstein, while likely aware of the experiment, later stated it wasn’t “dispositive” for his thinking. His path to special relativity was driven by deeper theoretical considerations, not solely experimental falsification. Even Hendrik Lorentz, who developed the mathematical transformations central to special relativity, interpreted them through the lens of ether-induced physical contractions and time dilations. It took decades for decisive experimental evidence, like the 1940s muon decay experiments—where muons were observed to decay slower than expected, precisely as special relativity predicted time dilation—to definitively favor Einstein’s kinematic interpretation over Lorentz’s dynamical, ether-based one.

The lesson here is profound: scientific progress rarely follows a clean, decisive falsification. Theories can coexist, interpretations can differ, and foundational beliefs can persist even in the face of contradictory evidence. The scientific community often adopts a “more correct” interpretation long before definitive experimental proof emerges, driven by subtle aesthetic or unifying principles that are hard to articulate.

The Prison of Expertise: When Knowing Too Much Holds Us Back

The individual journey of discovery is equally fraught with complexity. Nielsen highlights the fascinating case of Henri Poincaré, one of the greatest scientists of his era. Poincaré grasped many core principles that Einstein used for special relativity, including the principle that physics laws are consistent across inertial frames and the constant speed of light. Yet, he clung to a “dynamical” explanation for length contraction, believing it was caused by external forces, rather than Einstein’s revolutionary insight that space and time themselves were fundamentally different.

“It’s almost like he knew too much,” Nielsen muses. “He had almost too grand a vision in mind.” Einstein, in contrast, “subtracts from that and says, ‘No. Space and time are just different than what we thought, and here’s the correct picture.’” Expertise, in this view, can become a “prison,” making it harder for established minds to shed ingrained assumptions. This pattern isn’t unique; even Einstein later struggled with quantum mechanics and cosmology due to his own attachments.

The Long Road to Truth: From Aristarchus to Darwin

The history of science is replete with examples of ideas that took centuries to gain acceptance, not due to lack of conceptual simplicity, but due to a tangled web of verification challenges and cultural biases.

Consider heliocentrism. Aristarchus proposed the Earth orbits the Sun in the 2nd century BC. It was dismissed because, if true, stellar parallax (observable shifts in star positions as the Earth moves) should be visible. Since it wasn’t, the stars must be impossibly far away—a notion deemed absurd. It took until 1838 for stellar parallax to be experimentally measured. Humanity didn’t wait nearly two millennia for this validation to embrace Copernicus’s heliocentric model, even though Copernicus’s initial model was, in some ways, less accurate and more complex (requiring additional epicycles) than the Ptolemaic model it sought to replace.

What made Copernicus’s theory compelling then? Nielsen points to the power of unification, exemplified by Isaac Newton. Newton’s theory of gravitation not only explained planetary motion but also terrestrial motion (e.g., objects falling in parabolas) and the tides. Three seemingly disconnected phenomena were elegantly explained by one set of ideas. As John Maynard Keynes famously described Newton, he was “not the first of the age of reason. He was the last of the magicians, the last great mind which looked out on the visible and intellectual world with the same eyes as those who began to build our intellectual inheritance rather less than ten thousand years ago.” This suggests a blend of intuition, even “magical” thinking, alongside rigorous method, in the forging of new scientific paradigms.

Then there’s Darwin’s theory of natural selection. Conceptually, it seems “easier” than Newtonian gravity; Thomas Huxley famously remarked, “How extremely stupid not to have thought of this.” Yet, it took much longer to gain widespread acceptance. Darwin’s genius wasn’t just in the idea itself (parts of which were known to animal breeders or even hinted at by Lucretius in the 1st century BC), but in understanding its centrality to all biology. He compiled an overwhelming body of evidence, meticulously connecting it to geology and other fields, making an undeniable case for its pervasive relevance across the biosphere.

The delayed acceptance of Darwinism also highlights the need for critical “building blocks.” The geological discovery of “deep time” by Lyell in the 1830s was crucial; without millions of years, gradual evolution wouldn’t be plausible. Similarly, the age of colonization provided vast biogeographical data. This confluence of factors, Nielsen suggests, explains the near-simultaneous, independent discovery of natural selection by both Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace in the mid-19th century—a testament to ideas waiting for their historical moment.

AI and the New Frontier of Scientific Explanation

How do these insights into scientific progress inform the age of AI? The success of models like AlphaFold, which accurately predicts protein structures, is often cited as a triumph of AI in science. However, Nielsen offers a crucial nuance: AlphaFold’s success is “a story of data acquisition principally.” The several billion dollars spent over decades to build the Protein Data Bank through X-ray diffraction, NMR, and cryo-EM provided the massive dataset upon which AlphaFold was trained. The AI component, while impressive, represents a “tiny fraction of the entire investment.”

This raises a pivotal question: Can AI models like AlphaFold be considered scientific “explanations” in the classical sense? A traditional scientific explanation seeks deep, parsimonious principles, expressed in simple models with few free parameters. AlphaFold, with its hundreds of millions of parameters encoding complex, often uninterpretable relationships, doesn’t fit this mold.

Nielsen offers three possibilities:

  1. The Conservative View: AlphaFold is a useful model but not a scientific explanation.
  2. The Intermediate View: AlphaFold contains “lots of little explanations” that can be extracted through interpretability work. Just as experts have extracted chess strategies from AlphaZero that influenced Magnus Carlsen, we might “archeologically” mine AlphaFold for deeper biological principles.
  3. The Most Interesting Possibility: AlphaFold represents a new type of object for scientific inquiry. Like complex equations in Mathematica that were once intractable but now manipulable, these large models are new entities that we need new “verbs”—new operations like merging or distilling—to work with. They might not be explanations themselves, but sources from which explanations can be derived or entirely new forms of scientific understanding can emerge.

The challenge lies in whether AI can move beyond merely adding “epicycles” to existing models (like Ptolemy’s ever-growing complexity) to achieve revolutionary “flips” in understanding (like Copernicus’s heliocentrism or Einstein’s relativity). Raw gradient descent might optimize for local accuracy, but true paradigm shifts often involve sacrificing local accuracy for a globally simpler, more powerful theory. Einstein’s realization that Newtonian gravity allowed faster-than-light signaling, violating special relativity, was such a “forcing function”—a recognition that the old explanation was insufficient, demanding a fundamentally new approach.

The Indispensable Role of Diversity and Resilience

Ultimately, the history of science underscores the critical importance of diverse research programs. The contrasting stories of Uranus and Mercury illustrate this perfectly. Both planets exhibited orbital anomalies. For Uranus, the anomaly led to the successful prediction and discovery of Neptune, a triumph for Newtonian mechanics. For Mercury, similar predictions of an inner planet (“Vulcan”) proved fruitless; its anomaly required the radical shift to general relativity. A priori, scientists couldn’t know which approach would yield truth. They had to pursue both.

This difficulty with falsificationism is a recurring theme. When the Pioneer spacecraft exhibited anomalous deceleration, some eagerly posited new physics. The mundane truth, discovered later, was a slight asymmetry in thermal radiation. Most “exceptions” turn out to be mundane; only rarely do they point to fundamental flaws. The scientific community needs “remnants”—individuals or groups who persist in defending theories even when evidence seems hostile, as with Prout’s hypothesis of whole-number atomic weights, which was “falsified” for 85 years by chlorine’s 35.5 atomic weight, only to be vindicated by the discovery of isotopes.

The Shifting Bottlenecks and Alien Tech Stacks

AI will undoubtedly accelerate science by tackling well-understood domains with tight verification loops. However, it will also shift the bottlenecks. Programmers, now less constrained by coding time, find themselves bottlenecked by the generation of “interesting design ideas”—a domain where clear verification loops are absent.

This leads to Nielsen’s fascinating conjecture: if we met aliens, they might possess a “totally different technological stack than us.” We often assume that science is a singular, convergent path—a “theory of everything” for physics implies the end of discovery. But as computer science, which began with a “theory of everything” (Turing’s computation theory) in the 1930s, has shown, the subsequent ninety years have been an endless exploration of its consequences and the development of myriad new ideas.

The “science and tech tree,” Nielsen suggests, is probably far vaster than we realize. Our current path is just one branch, shaped by our specific history, biases, and the sequence of our discoveries. Other civilizations, with different starting points, intellectual aesthetics, or environmental pressures, might have explored entirely different branches, leading to wholly unique scientific understandings and technological capabilities.

In conclusion, while AI promises to be a powerful engine for scientific progress, the journey of discovery remains deeply human. It requires not just the systematic application of methods, but also intuition, aesthetic judgment, the resilience to pursue diverse and seemingly “wrong” ideas, and the courage to question fundamental assumptions. The true acceleration of science in the age of AI will depend not just on automating existing processes, but on fostering the very human qualities that have, throughout history, allowed us to navigate the mysterious and elusive path toward understanding the universe.

The AI Paradox: Why Your Next Promotion is to CEO (and How to Get There)

In an era consumed by anxieties over artificial intelligence displacing human workers, a provocative new perspective emerges: AI won’t take your job; it will, in fact, make you the CEO. This isn’t a utopian fantasy but a pragmatic vision of a human-machine synthesis, where human agency and discernment become more valuable than ever, transforming our roles from task-doers to strategic orchestrators.

According to a leading expert featured on the a16z show, the core of this transformation lies in understanding AI as a powerful, yet fundamentally limited, “shortcut.” While shortcuts can be incredibly efficient, their true value is unlocked only by those who understand “the long way around.” Without this foundational knowledge, one cannot debug, verify, or truly leverage AI’s capabilities. It’s the difference between memorizing a complex mathematical identity like Euler’s ($e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$) and being able to derive it from first principles. The latter empowers you to truly understand and apply the former.

The Decentralized Future: AI’s Economy of Distillation

The prevailing narrative often pits tech giants, with their vast capital and computing power, as the inevitable monopolizers of the AI economy. However, the expert posits a different future: one dominated by distillation and decentralization.

“Distillation attacks” are key to this shift. These techniques allow smaller, more efficient models to be created by querying large foundational models, often at a fraction of the cost (up to 98% cheaper). This makes it incredibly difficult for large labs to maintain exclusive control over their models. The analogy is striking: just as Google “scraped the entire internet” to build its index, or Facebook “scraped Harvard social networks,” these large models are built on vast public data. Preventing others from “distilling” that knowledge becomes ethically and technically challenging.

This economic shift paves the way for a future characterized by personal, private, and programmable AI. The sheer power of AI, the expert argues, necessitates its use within “trusted tribes” – closed networks where information can be shared and processed without fear of broader exposure.

The Commons as a “Hall of Mirrors”: Why Privacy Matters More

The rise of AI brings with it an unprecedented ability to synthesize information across massive datasets. A chilling example cited is the “Jmail thing” – the ability to query thousands of emails, like those of Jeffrey Epstein, to build a comprehensive narrative. What was once “secure through obscurity” is now instantly discoverable.

This capability leads to what the French call sousveillance – surveillance from below, or a “Bentham panopticon” where everyone is watching everyone else. Any public information, once indexed by AI, can be used for stalking, manipulation, or worse. The inevitable consequence, the expert suggests, is that “the commons becomes a hall of mirrors with all kinds of pseudonyms, and so people retreat back to caves and tribes.”

Within these trusted tribes, AI dramatically increases productivity. However, outside them, the digital landscape risks becoming a wasteland of “AI spam” – low-quality emails, replies, and presentations.

The “AI Slop” Problem: Lazy, Stupid, or Evil?

The expert’s reaction to seeing AI-generated content in a slide deck is visceral: “No. No, actually the opposite.” Despite advancements, AI-generated text or images often possess a “generic look,” much like someone who hasn’t bothered to change their computer’s default desktop wallpaper. This “default AI” is immediately recognizable.

When confronted with such “AI slop,” the expert’s interpretation is stark: the creator is either lazy, stupid, or evil.

  • Lazy: They didn’t invest the time to make the content concise and impactful, instead opting for a verbose, undigested output. (Echoing Mark Twain’s famous apology: “I didn’t have time to write you a short letter, so I wrote you a long one.”)
  • Stupid: They genuinely don’t understand that discerning humans can instantly differentiate between authentic, thoughtful work and AI-generated filler.
  • Evil: They are attempting to deceive, to pass off subpar or undiligent work as their own.

This strong reaction, coming from a self-professed “pro-tech, pro-AI” individual, underscores a critical point: AI reduces the cost of generation but increases the cost of verification. In a world where AI can effortlessly craft a polished resume, the effort required to verify its authenticity skyrockets. This dynamic will create a surge in demand for new jobs in “proctoring and verification,” as companies resort to in-person interviews and proctored offline exams to ensure genuine skill.

The Chinese Internet Analogy: Digital Autarky

The expert draws a fascinating parallel between the future of AI and the evolution of the Chinese internet. In a “low-trust society,” Chinese companies historically avoided Software as a Service (SaaS) models, preferring to build everything in-house due to concerns about data privacy and intellectual property. This led to a “digital autarky” – a self-sufficient digital ecosystem, albeit with the frictional costs of reinventing the wheel and less division of labor.

With AI, even non-Chinese companies can now achieve a similar level of “digital autarky.” The “build versus buy” question shifts, allowing organizations to create more powerful internal tools, bypassing external SaaS providers and their inherent trust requirements.

AI’s Sweet Spot: The Verifiable and the Physical

Not all tasks are created equal for AI. The expert highlights specific areas where AI excels due to ease of verification:

  • Visuals over Verbal: Our brains are “built-in GPUs,” adept at instantly spotting anomalies in images or video (e.g., messed-up hands, janky user interfaces). Visual verification is relatively cheap and intuitive.
  • The Physical World: Unlike the fluid digital realm, there is “only one physical world.” Tasks like moving a box from one pallet to another are unequivocally verifiable. This makes the physical world a prime candidate for AI automation, akin to self-driving cars eventually achieving near 100% reliability. This is where “Chinese-style physical AI” (robotics) will thrive.

Conversely, the digital world presents greater verification challenges due to its fuzzy boundaries and subjective nature. How do you know when a digital “to-do list” is truly “done,” compared to the clear completion of moving 100 physical boxes?

This leads to a crucial rule for the AI age: “No public undisclosed AI.” Just as some cultures ban alcohol due to an inability to “hold their liquor,” some will become “AI totalers,” swearing off AI entirely if they cannot trust its origin or intent. The expert notes that even for tech-savvy individuals like Nate Silver, the act of prompting AI and verifying its output can often be slower than performing the task manually. AI, in this sense, does “middle to middle,” not end-to-end, requiring significant human oversight.

Beyond Verbal Prompts: AI Can Read Your Body, If Not Your Mind

The idea of AI reading our minds often ventures into science fiction. However, the expert suggests a more plausible and imminent reality: “I’m not sure whether AI will be able to read your mind, but it can read your body.”

Our bodies are constantly generating a vast stream of “sensor data” – gene expression levels, clinical lab results (bilirubin, hematocrit), wearable data. This “telemetry,” as explored by Stanford professor Mike Snyder’s “integrum” concept, can reveal subtle physiological changes, like impending illness, even before we experience symptoms. This continuous, non-verbal data stream could serve as a powerful new way to “prompt” AI, guiding its actions based on our biological state and needs, without explicit verbal commands.

AI as the “Rise of Asia and India” (Again)

To illustrate AI’s transformative scale, the expert offers another compelling analogy: AI is like “the rise of Asia and India” from an American perspective. The emergence of a billion Chinese and a billion Indians provided immense capacity for physical manufacturing and digital outsourcing. Similarly, we are now witnessing the arrival of “a billion factory robots and a billion digital agents.” This vast new workforce can execute tasks with unprecedented efficiency, but it still requires human intellect to define the “product” – to articulate what needs to be done.

The Human Edge: Sensing Markets and Politics

While AI excels at pattern recognition and execution, it fundamentally struggles with domains characterized by adversarial dynamics and time-variance: markets and politics.

Unlike identifying a dog or playing chess (where rules are constant), markets are inherently adversarial. A trading strategy that works today will be countered tomorrow. “It’s not a time-invariant thing,” the expert explains, and it’s “multiplayer” – whatever move you make, someone else is adapting. If everyone uses the same AI models for market analysis, the true edge will come from being non-AI, from human intuition and novel strategies. Generic tools yield no specific advantage. The same applies to politics, where public interest, timely topics, and narratives constantly shift.

This reinforces the core thesis: “Humans are the sensor. AI is the actuator.” Humans sense the complex, dynamic, and adversarial conditions of the world – financial, market, political – and then translate that understanding into cleanly articulated English prompts. The AI then acts upon these instructions. “Taste,” often cited as a uniquely human quality, is fundamentally “a sense” – an ability to perceive and discern, which AI cannot yet replicate.

Debunking the Overlords: AI is Designed for the Leash

The pervasive fear of AI overlords or a singular, god-like AGI is, the expert argues, largely a product of science fiction. Economically useful AI is “designed for the leash.” It starts when prompted, performs its task, and then stops. If it didn’t, it would be inefficient and costly, “burning tokens” and resources. Even in China, where physical robots are being rapidly deployed, they are kept on a tight leash, just as humans are.

The concept of a truly autonomous, self-replicating Skynet scenario is highly improbable due to fundamental “frictional breaks.” For AI to reproduce itself, it would need to command physical robots to mine ore, construct data centers, manufacture chips, and manage an entire supply chain. This complex, resource-intensive process would be easily detectable and stoppable. Furthermore, humans have strong economic incentives to implement safeguards (like “off switches” and cryptographic keys) to prevent such scenarios, much like we regulate electricity to prevent widespread electrocution.

Instead of a monotheistic AGI god, we are more likely to see “polytheistic” decentralized AIs. The fear of AI, the expert suggests, often comes from humans projecting their own anxieties onto the powerful tools they themselves create.

The CEO in All of Us: Redefining Human Work

So, if AI is on a leash and humans are the sensors, what does the future of work look like? It means a fundamental redefinition of roles, where “AI makes you the CEO.”

While some jobs will be fully automated (like elevator operators or artisan chair makers), the nature of work shifts. The artisan doesn’t disappear; they evolve into the “manager” who designs the chair factory and the “technician” who debugs it. Similarly, in the AI era, humans become the “engineering manager” who writes the prompts and the “technician” who verifies the output.

AI will revolutionize fields like biology by synthesizing vast amounts of existing literature, accelerating discovery within known parameters. However, truly novel scientific breakthroughs and mathematical proofs, while aided by AI, still require human expertise to formulate the initial prompt and, crucially, to verify the result. AI는 ‘무엇을(What)’ 할지 아는 데 도움을 주지만, ‘왜(Why)’ 해야 하는지를 정의하고 소통하는 것은 인간 리더의 몫입니다.

  3. 실질적인 피드백 제공 (Providing actionable feedback): AI는 성과 데이터를 분석하고 객관적인 지표를 제시할 수 있습니다. 특정 업무에서 어떤 부분이 미흡했는지, 어떤 지표가 개선되어야 하는지 등에 대한 분석은 AI가 훨씬 정확하고 빠르게 수행할 수 있습니다. 하지만 개인의 성장과 발전을 위한 건설적이고 공감 어린 피드백, 즉 인간적인 이해를 바탕으로 한 조언은 오직 인간만이 줄 수 있습니다. 섬세한 감정적 지지와 동기 부여, 그리고 복합적인 상황을 고려한 맞춤형 조언은 팀원들의 잠재력을 최대한 끌어내는 데 필수적입니다. AI가 ‘무엇이 잘못되었는지’를 알려준다면, 인간 매니저는 ‘어떻게 개선하고 성장할 수 있는지’를 함께 고민하고 격려하는 역할을 합니다.

“Michael Nielsen – Why aliens will have a different tech stack than us” — Dwarkesh Patel 기반 기사 원본 영상 보기

아인슈타인도 몰랐던 과학적 진보의 복잡한 진실: AI 시대, 우리는 과학을 어떻게 이해해야 하는가?

오늘날 인공지능(AI)은 과학적 발견의 속도를 가속화할 잠재력을 지닌 도구로 주목받고 있습니다. AI가 과학적 발견의 ‘검증 루프(verification loop)‘를 닫는 데 기여할 것이라는 기대 속에서, 우리는 과연 과학적 진보란 무엇이며, 이를 어떻게 인식하고 있는가에 대한 근본적인 질문에 직면합니다. 양자 컴퓨팅의 선구자이자 딥러닝 분야에 지대한 영향을 미친 마이클 닐슨(Michael Nielsen)은 드와케시 파텔(Dwarkesh Patel)과의 대화에서 우리가 흔히 아는 ‘과학적 방법론’의 단순한 서사를 뒤집으며, 과학적 진보가 인간의 역사만큼이나 미스터리하고 복잡한 힘에 의해 추동된다는 통찰을 제시합니다.

미켈슨-몰리 실험과 상대성 이론의 재해석: 통념을 넘어서

미켈슨-몰리(Michelson-Morley) 실험은 종종 아인슈타인(Einstein)의 특수 상대성 이론(Special Theory of Relativity) 탄생에 결정적인 역할을 한 실험으로 알려져 있습니다. 1880년대에 수행된 이 실험이 에테르(ether)의 존재를 부정하여 물리학에 위기를 초래했고, 아인슈타인이 이를 특수 상대성 이론으로 해결했다는 것이 일반적인 이야기입니다. 그러나 마이클 닐슨은 아브라함 파이스(Abraham Pais)의 아인슈타인 전기와 임레 라카토슈(Imre Lakatos)의 ‘과학 연구 프로그램의 방법론(The Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes)‘을 인용하며, 이 통념과는 다른 이야기를 들려줍니다.

1600년대 로버트 보일(Robert Boyle)이 처음 제안한 에테르 개념은 빛이 소리처럼 어떤 매질의 진동이라고 가정한 데서 출발했습니다. 보일은 진공에서도 빛이 전파됨을 발견하고, 이 보이지 않는 매질을 에테르라고 불렀습니다. 이후 200여 년간 에테르의 본질에 대한 다양한 이론들이 논의되었고, 미켈슨-몰리 실험은 이러한 에테르 이론들, 특히 ‘에테르 바람(ether wind)‘의 존재를 시험하기 위한 것이었습니다. 지구의 움직임에 따라 에테르 바람이 발생하고, 이에 따라 빛의 속도가 달라질 것이라는 가설을 검증하려 한 것입니다.

놀랍게도 실험 결과는 에테르 바람이 없다는 것을 보여주었지만, 이는 에테르 이론 중 일부를 배제했을 뿐, 모든 에테르 이론을 부정하지는 못했습니다. 심지어 실험의 주역인 미켈슨 자신도 1920년대까지 에테르의 존재를 믿고 관련 실험을 계속했습니다. 아인슈타인 역시 나중에 자신이 미켈슨-몰리 논문을 알고 있었는지조차 확신하지 못했다고 밝혔을 정도로, 이 실험은 그의 사고에 결정적인 영향을 미치지 않았습니다.

이 시기 네덜란드의 물리학자 헨드릭 로렌츠(Hendrik Lorentz)는 수학적으로는 올바른 변환식(로렌츠 변환, Lorentz transformations)을 도출했습니다. 이는 특수 상대성 이론의 기초가 되었지만, 로렌츠는 이를 에테르를 기준으로 한 좌표계와 그에 상대적으로 움직이는 다른 좌표계 간의 변환으로 해석했습니다. 즉, 길이 수축(length contraction)과 시간 지연(time dilation)을 에테르를 통한 운동의 동역학적 효과로 본 것입니다.

이러한 로렌츠의 해석과 특수 상대성 이론은 초기에는 실험적으로 구별하기 어려웠습니다. 그러나 1940년대에 시작된 뮤온(muon) 실험은 우주선이 대기 상층부에 충돌하여 생성된 뮤온이 예상보다 훨씬 느리게 붕괴한다는 것을 보여주었습니다. 이는 고전 이론으로는 설명할 수 없지만, 특수 상대성 이론에 따라 뮤온의 시간이 실제로 느려졌다면 완벽하게 들어맞았습니다. 이처럼 과학 공동체는 실험적 증거가 명확히 나오기 전에도 더 ‘올바른’ 해석을 채택하는 과정이 있음을 시사합니다. 과학적 진보는 단순한 ‘반증(falsification)‘만으로 설명되지 않는 복잡한 힘에 의해 작동하며, 위대한 과학자들조차도 기존의 신념에서 벗어나지 못하는 경우가 많습니다.

위대한 과학자들의 딜레마: 푸앵카레와 전문성의 함정

프랑스의 위대한 수학자이자 물리학자인 앙리 푸앵카레(Henri Poincaré)의 사례는 ‘올바른 질문’을 던졌지만 ‘결정적인 통찰’에 도달하지 못한 과학적 진보의 또 다른 흥미로운 단면을 보여줍니다. 푸앵카레는 모든 관성 좌표계(inertial reference frame)에서 물리 법칙이 동일하다는 상대성 원리(principle of relativity)와 빛의 속도가 일정하다는 개념을 아인슈타인과 거의 동시에 이해했습니다. 이는 아인슈타인이 특수 상대성 이론을 도출하는 데 사용한 핵심 아이디어들이었습니다.

그러나 푸앵카레는 길이 수축을 입자들이 외부 힘에 의해 압축되는 ‘동역학적 효과(dynamical effect)‘로 이해하는 데 머물렀습니다. 반면 아인슈타인은 이를 공간과 시간이 우리가 생각했던 것과는 근본적으로 다르다는 ‘운동학적 효과(kinematics)‘로 파악하고, 기존의 개념을 ‘빼는’ 방식으로 새로운 그림을 제시했습니다. 닐슨은 푸앵카레의 방대한 전문 지식이 오히려 새로운 관점을 받아들이는 데 방해가 되었을 수 있다고 추측합니다. 너무 많은 것을 알고 이해했기 때문에 기존의 사고방식을 내려놓지 못했다는 것입니다. 아인슈타인 역시 양자역학이나 우주론의 올바른 해석을 받아들이는 데 어려움을 겪었던 사례는, 위대한 과학자들조차도 자신의 전문성과 기존의 관념에 ‘갇힐’ 수 있음을 보여줍니다. 과학적 진보가 특정 ‘절차’에 따라 이루어지는 것이 아니라, 때로는 기존 지식의 무게에서 벗어나는 용기가 필요함을 시사합니다.

첫째, AI는 대규모 데이터 세트에서 작은 정보라도 놓치지 않습니다. 과거에는 ‘모호함을 통한 보안(security through obscurity)‘이 가능했던 정보들이 AI에 의해 쉽게 노출될 수 있습니다. 예를 들어, 제프리 엡스타인(Jeffrey Epstein)의 이메일처럼, 수천 개의 이메일에서 정보를 종합하여 이야기를 만들어내는 것이 가능해졌습니다. 이는 단순한 감시를 넘어 ‘아래로부터의 감시(Sousveillance)’ 또는 벤담의 ‘파놉티콘(Panopticon)‘처럼 모두가 서로를 감시하는 세상으로 이어질 수 있습니다. 공개된 모든 정보가 AI에 의해 인덱싱되고 검색 가능해지면서, 사람들은 서로를 스토킹하거나 악용할 수 있습니다.

이러한 상황은 디지털 공동체를 ‘거울의 방(hall of mirrors)‘으로 만들고, 사람들은 가명 뒤에 숨거나 결국 ‘동굴과 부족’으로 후퇴하게 될 것입니다. 신뢰할 수 있는 부족 내에서는 코드를 공유하고 협력하여 AI를 통해 생산성을 극대화할 수 있습니다. 그러나 부족 외부에서는 엄청난 양의 AI 스팸, 저품질 슬라이드, AI 생성 콘텐츠가 넘쳐날 것입니다.

검증의 역설: AI가 높이는 비용

AI는 콘텐츠 ‘생성(generation)‘의 비용을 획기적으로 낮추지만, 그 생성된 콘텐츠를 ‘검증(verification)‘하는 비용은 오히려 증가시킵니다. 이력서 작성은 AI 덕분에 쉬워졌지만, 이제 이력서를 검증하는 데는 더 많은 시간과 노력이 필요합니다. 과거에는 특정 어휘력과 경험이 있어야 잘 작성된 커버레터나 이력서를 만들 수 있었지만, 이제 AI는 그럴듯한 외형(chrome)을 쉽게 만들어낼 수 있기 때문입니다.

AI는 이러한 자급자족을 가능하게 하여, 비중국 기업들도 ‘내부 도구(internal tools)’ 개발에 집중하는 중국식 모델을 따를 수 있게 할 것입니다.

AI의 강점과 약점: 시각과 물리, 그리고 ‘몸’을 읽는 AI

AI는 현재 시점에서 ‘언어보다 시각(visuals over verbal)‘에 더 강점을 보입니다. 이미지나 비디오는 인간의 내장된 GPU(눈)로 쉽게 오류를 감지하고 수정할 수 있습니다. 손 모양이 이상하거나 사용자 경험(UX)이 조악한 웹페이지를 즉시 알아챌 수 있습니다. 반면, 백엔드 코드와 같은 복잡한 디지털 작업은 검증이 훨씬 어렵습니다. 아마존의 사례처럼, AI에 전적으로 의존한 자동화는 대규모 장애로 이어질 수 있습니다.

AI는 지름길이지만, 전문가만이 그 지름길을 효과적으로 사용할 수 있습니다. 복잡한 수학 공식을 암기하여 바로 답을 내놓을 수는 있지만, 그 공식을 첫 원리부터 증명하려면 복소 지수 함수의 정의 등 근본적인 지식을 알아야 합니다. AI 이전 세대가 이러한 ‘먼 길’을 배웠기 때문에 AI라는 지름길을 활용하고 디버깅할 수 있습니다. 그러나 ‘먼 길’을 모르는 세대에게 AI는 단순히 ‘모르는 것을 모르게 만드는’ 도구가 될 수 있습니다.

또한 AI는 ‘굴레에 매이도록(built for the harness)’ 설계되었습니다. 경제적으로 유용한 AI는 사용자의 프롬프트(prompt)에 정확히 반응하고, 지시가 없으면 멈춥니다. 물리적 세계의 AI(로봇)도 마찬가지입니다. 하나의 물리적 세계는 검증이 용이합니다. 자율주행차가 특정 위치에서 다른 위치로 100% 신뢰도로 이동하는 것처럼, 로봇이 상자를 옮기는 작업은 100% 검증 가능합니다. 그러나 디지털 세계는 개인의 구성된 환경이 다양하고, ‘해야 할 일 목록(to-do list)‘처럼 경계가 모호한 작업이 많아 검증이 훨씬 어렵습니다.

이 전문가는 “공개되지 않은 AI는 안 된다(No Public Undisclosed AI)“는 원칙을 강조합니다. AI에 대한 대중의 불신은 ‘AI 금주주의자(AI totalers)‘를 낳을 수 있습니다. 네이트 실버(Nate Silver)는 AI를 ‘도박(gamble)‘에 비유하며, 프롬프트 입력과 결과 검증 과정이 직접 하는 것보다 느릴 때도 많다고 지적합니다.

흥미로운 점은 AI가 ‘마음을 읽을 수 있을지’는 불확실하지만, ‘몸은 읽을 수 있다’는 주장입니다. 스탠퍼드 대학교 마이크 스나이더(Mike Snyder) 교수의 ‘인테그롬(Integrum)’ 연구처럼, 유전자 발현 데이터, 혈액 검사 결과 등 신체에서 나오는 방대한 생체 데이터(telemetry)는 우리가 의식하지 못하는 사이에 AI를 프롬프트할 수 있습니다. 착용형 기기(wearables)와 바이오 기술은 비언어적으로 AI에 명령을 내리는 새로운 방식을 가능하게 할 것입니다.

시장과 정치, AI의 한계

AI가 시장과 정치 같은 복잡한 영역을 감지하고 예측할 수 있을지에 대해서는 회의적인 시각을 보입니다. 시장은 ‘적대적(adversarial)‘이고 ‘시간 불변적(time-invariant)‘이지 않습니다. 개와 고양이를 구별하는 것과 달리, 시장에서는 특정 거래 전략이 성공하면 다른 참여자들이 이를 역이용하려 합니다. 모두가 같은 AI 모델을 사용한다면, AI를 사용하지 않는 것이 오히려 우위를 점하는 ‘엣지(edge)‘가 될 수 있습니다. 일반적인 도구로는 특정 이점을 얻을 수 없기 때문입니다.

마찬가지로 정치는 끊임없이 변화하는 주제와 시의성에 따라 달라지므로, AI가 이를 효과적으로 다루기 어렵습니다. 따라서 인간은 ‘센서(sensor)‘이고 AI는 ‘액추에이터(actuator)‘라는 비유가 중요합니다. 인간이 세상의 재정, 시장, 정치적 상황을 감지하고, 이를 명확한 영어 프롬프트로 AI에 전달하면 AI가 이를 실행합니다. 결국 ‘취향(taste)‘은 곧 ‘감각(sense)‘이며, AI는 아직 인간처럼 세상을 감지할 수 없습니다. AI는 ‘굴레에 매이도록’ 설계되었고, 통제 불가능한 자율성은 경제적 효용성이 떨어집니다.

AI는 ‘길들여진 도구’, 자율성은 신화인가?

AI가 궁극적으로 ‘신(god)‘과 같은 존재, 즉 범용인공지능(AGI)이 되어 인류를 지배할 것이라는 우려에 대해서는 회의적입니다. 오히려 AI는 ‘다신교적(polytheistic)‘인 형태로 분산될 것이며, 물리적 AI(로봇)는 ‘노예(slaves)‘처럼 활용될 것이라고 말합니다. 이는 다소 도발적인 표현이지만, AI가 인간의 통제를 벗어나기 어렵다는 점을 강조합니다.

WSO는 기내에서 치료를 받았고, 조종사와 함께 독일의 의료 병원으로 이송될 예정이었습니다. 이 성공적인 구출 작전은 트럼프 대통령에게 엄청난 정치적 승리(huge victory)를 안겨주었고, 그는 백악관 기자회견에서 이를 대대적으로 선전했습니다. 트럼프 대통령은 이 작전을 “군 역사상 가장 크고, 가장 복잡하며, 가장 끔찍했던 전투 수색 및 구조 임무”라고 칭송하며, 미군과 CIA의 기술과 정밀함, 치명적인 무력에 대해 찬사를 보냈습니다. 그는 미국 군대만이 이런 놀라운 작전을 성공시킬 수 있었다고 강조했습니다.

하지만 에릭 슈미트 기자는 이러한 승리에도 불구하고 전쟁은 여전히 인기가 없으며, 전 세계와 미국에 경제적 파장을 일으키고 있다고 지적합니다. 또한 대통령의 많은 전략적 목표가 아직 달성되지 않았다고 덧붙입니다.

이란 역시 다른 이유로 이번 사건을 큰 승리로 선전했습니다. 그들은 미국 전투기를 격추하는 데 성공했으며, 이는 미국이 이란 상공을 완전히 통제하지 못한다는 것을 증명했다고 주장했습니다. 이란은 여전히 매일 1530발의 탄도 미사일(ballistic missiles)과 50100대의 일방향 공격 드론(one-way attack drones)을 발사할 수 있는 상당한 능력을 보유하고 있음을 보여주었습니다. 이는 이란이 여전히 싸울 의지와 능력을 가지고 있음을 의미합니다.

전쟁의 출구 전략과 불확실한 미래

이러한 상황은 전쟁의 출구 전략(off-ramp)에 대한 의문을 제기합니다. 전쟁 6주차에 많은 사람들이 미국이 이란의 군사력을 상당 부분 파괴했다고 생각했지만, 현실은 달랐습니다. 미국과 이스라엘은 이란의 미사일 프로그램, 드론 프로그램, 해군, 공군을 상당 부분 파괴하거나 약화시키는 데 성공했습니다. 그러나 이란 군대는 여전히 탄력적이며 미사일과 드론을 발사할 수 있는 능력을 유지하고 있습니다.

정치적 목표는 여전히 불확실합니다. 트럼프 대통령은 자신의 최종 목표에 대해 모순된 설명을 내놓고 있으며, 구출 작전의 성공을 선전하면서도 이란에 대해 격렬하게 비난하며 위협했습니다. 그는 이란이 화요일 밤까지 휴전 제안을 받아들이지 않으면, 국가 전체를 하룻밤 만에 파괴할 수 있다고 협박하며 교량, 발전소 등 인프라(infrastructure) 공격을 경고했습니다.

일부 전문가들은 이런 위협이 오히려 트럼프 대통령에게 구출 작전의 성공이 자신감을 불어넣어 더 대담해지게 만들었다고 분석합니다. 그의 위협은 군사적 목표를 넘어 수백만 민간인에게 영향을 미칠 인프라를 겨냥하고 있으며, 많은 법률 전문가들은 이러한 공격이 국제법에 위배될 수 있다고 경고합니다.

구출 작전 이전에는 전쟁의 출구 전략에 대한 논의가 많았습니다. 미국과 이란 모두 현재 승리를 주장하고 있으니, 이것이 혹시 협상으로 가는 길은 아닐까요? 트럼프 대통령은 여전히 외교적 해결을 원한다고 말하고 있으며, 이란은 월요일 호르무즈 해협의 안전 통행 프로토콜(protocol)과 이란에 대한 제재 해제(lifting of sanctions)를 포함하는 새로운 10개항 제안을 제출했습니다. 하지만 전문가들은 협상이 아직 멀었다고 보고 있습니다.

현재 트럼프 대통령은 이란이 휴전 제안을 받아들이지 않으면 인프라에 대한 공격에 직면할 것이라는 최후통첩을 보낸 상태입니다. 희망적으로 본다면 양측이 모종의 해결책을 찾을 수도 있겠지만, 아직 핵심 쟁점들에 대한 이견이 크고, 대통령은 오히려 확전(escalation) 방향으로 나아가려는 것처럼 보입니다.

영웅 서사와 냉혹한 현실

이 구출 작전은 할리우드 영화에서나 나올 법한 영웅 서사(heroic narrative)로 가득했습니다. 그리고 이 이야기는 행정부가 미국 대중에게 전쟁을 설득하는 데 어려움을 겪고 있던 시기에 나왔습니다. 대중은 치솟는 유가와 전쟁의 명분에 의문을 제기하며 피로감을 느끼고 있었습니다. 백악관은 미국의 군사력, 독창성, 용기를 보여주는 이 믿을 수 없을 정도로 영웅적인 이야기를 최대한 활용하려 할 것입니다.

하지만 이 작전의 성공이 아무리 영화 같고 드라마틱하더라도, 남겨진 현실은 냉혹합니다. 호르무즈 해협은 여전히 봉쇄되어 경제적 어려움이 지속되고 있고, 이란 강경 정부는 스스로가 승리하고 있다고 믿으며, 이스파한 지하에 묻힌 수천 파운드의 고농축 우라늄(highly-enriched uranium) 문제는 해결되지 않았습니다. 트럼프 대통령은 이란의 핵무기 획득을 막는 것이 여전히 그의 주된 목표라고 말했지만, 이를 달성하기 위한 구체적인 목표는 제시하지 않았습니다.

결론적으로, 이란 적진에서의 에어맨 구출 작전은 전술적 측면에서 놀라운 성공을 거두었지만, 미국 행정부는 여전히 해결해야 할 많은 난제들을 안고 있습니다. 이 영웅적인 이야기가 전쟁의 본질적인 문제들을 가릴 수 있을지는 미지수입니다.
